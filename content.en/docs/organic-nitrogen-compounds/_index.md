## INTRODUCTION

Organic compounds containing nitrogen are essential to life. For example; amines, the organic derivatives of ammonia play an important role in bioregulation, neurotransmission, etc. Pyridoxine, Vitamin {{< katex >}}\mathbf{B}_6{{< /katex >}} is an organic nitrogen compound which is needed to maintain the health of nerves, skin and red blood cells. Plants synthesise alkaloids, and biologically active amines to protect them from being eaten away by insects and other animals. Diazonium salts finds important applications in synthetic organic chemistry. Nitrogen compounds are the important constituents of explosives, drugs, dyes, fuels, polymers, synthetic rubbers, etc.

## 13.1 NITRO COMPOUNDS

### 13.1.1 Classification of nitrocompounds

Nitro compounds are considered as the derivatives of hydrocarbons. If one of the hydrogen atom of hydrocarbon is replaced by the - {{< katex >}}NO_2{{< /katex >}} group, the resultant organic compound is called a nitrocompound.

#### 13.1.2 Nomenclature of nitroalkanes

In the IUPAC nomenclature, the nitroalkanes are named by adding prefix nitro before the name of alkane, the position of the nitro group is indicated by number.

| Compound (common name, Structural formula, IUPAC Name) | IUPAC Name |
|:---|:---|
| CH3CH-CH2-NO2, CH3 (2-Methyl-1-nitropropane) | 2-Methyl-1-nitropropane |
| CH3-C-CH2-NO2, CH3 (2,2-dimethyl-1-nitropropane) | 2,2-dimethyl-1-nitropropane |
| Nitrobenzene | nitrobenzene |
| CH3, NO2 (2-nitro-1-methylbenzene) | 2-nitro-1-methylbenzene |
| 1,3,5-trinitrobenzene | 1,3,5-trinitrobenzene |
| CH2-CH2-NO2 (2-phenyl-1-nitroethane) | 2-phenyl-1-nitroethane |

#### 13.1.3 ISOMERISM

Nitroalkanes exhibit chain and position isomerism among their own class and functional isomerism with alkyl nitrites and special type tautomerism can also exist in nitro alkanes having an {{< katex >}}\alpha{{< /katex >}} - H atom. For example, nitro compounds having the molecular formula {{< katex >}}\mathrm{C_4H_9NO_2}{{< /katex >}} exhibit the following isomerisms.

| Isomerism | Structural formula of isomers |
|:---|:---|
| Chain isomerism: They differ in the length of carbon chain. | CH3CH2CH2CH2—NO2 and CH3CHCH2—NO2 (1-nitrobutane) (2-methyl-1-nitropropane) |
| Position isomerism: They differ in the position of nitro group. | CH3CH2CH2CH2—NO2 and CH3CHCH2CH3 (1-nitrobutane) NO2 (2-nitrobutane) |
| Functional isomerism: Nitroalkanes exhibit functional isomerism with alkyl nitrites | CH3CH2CH2CH2—NO2 and CH3CH2CH2CH2—O—N=O (butyl nitrite) |

**Tautomerism:** Primary and secondary nitroalkanes, having {{< katex >}}\alpha{{< /katex >}} - H, also show an equilibrium mixture of two tautomers namely nitro - and aci - form

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Tautomerism in nitroalkanes</center>

Tertiary nitro alkanes do not exhibit tautomerism due to absence of {{< katex >}}\alpha{{< /katex >}} - H atom.

| S.No. | Nitro form | Aci – form |
|:---|:---|:---|
| 1. | Less acidic | More acidic |
| 2. | Dissolves in NaOH slowly | Dissolves in NaOH instantly |
| 3. | Decolourises FeCl3 solution | With FeCl3 gives reddish brown colour |
| 4. | Electrical conductivity is low | Electrical conductivity is high |

#### 13.1.4 Acidic nature of nitro alkanes

The {{< katex >}}\alpha{{< /katex >}} - H atom of {{< katex >}}1^{\circ}{{< /katex >}} & {{< katex >}}2^{\circ}{{< /katex >}} nitroalkanes show acidic character because of the electron withdrawing effect of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NO}_{2}{{< /katex >}} group. These are more acidic than aldehydes, ketones, ester and cyanides. Nitroalkanes dissolve in NaOH solution to form a salt. Aci - nitro derivatives are more acidic than nitro form. When the number of alkyl group attached to {{< katex >}}\alpha{{< /katex >}} carbon increases, acidity decreases due to {{< katex >}}+1{{< /katex >}} effect of alkyl groups.

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{CH}_{3} - \mathrm{NO}_{2} > \mathrm{CH}_{3}\mathrm{CH}_{2} - \mathrm{NO}_{2} > \mathrm{CH}_{3}\mathrm{NO}_{2} > \mathrm{CH}_{3}\mathrm{NO}_{2}{{< /katex >}}

#### 13.1.5 Preparation of nitroalkanes

**1) From alkyl halides: (Laboratory method)**

a) Alkyl bromides (or) iodides on heating with ethanolic solution of potassium nitrite gives nitroethane.

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{CH}_{3}\mathrm{CH}_{2} - \mathrm{Br} + \mathrm{KNO}_{2}\xrightarrow{\mathrm{ethanol / \Delta}} \mathrm{CH}_{3}\mathrm{CH}_{2} - \mathrm{NO}_{2} + \mathrm{KBr}{{< /katex >}}

The reaction follows SN2 mechanism.

This method is not suitable for preparing nitrobenzene because the bromine directly attached to the benzene ring cannot be cleaved easily.

**2) Vapour phase nitration of alkanes: (Industrial method)**

Gaseous mixture of methane and nitric acid passed through a red hot metal tube to give nitromethane.

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{CH}_{4}(\mathrm{g}) + \mathrm{HNO}_{3}\xrightarrow[675\mathrm{K}]{\mathrm{Red\ hot\ tube}}\mathrm{CH}_{3}\mathrm{-NO}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}{{< /katex >}}

Except methane, other alkanes (upto {{< katex >}}\mathrm{n}{{< /katex >}} - hexane) give a mixture of nitroalkanes due to C-C cleavage. The individual nitro alkanes can be separated by fractional distillation.

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{CH}_{3} - \mathrm{CH}_{3} + \mathrm{HNO}_{3}\xrightarrow[675\mathrm{K}]{\mathrm{}}\mathrm{CH}_{3}\mathrm{CH}_{2} - \mathrm{NO}_{2} + \mathrm{CH}_{3}\mathrm{NO}_{2}{{< /katex >}}

**3) From {{< katex >}}\alpha{{< /katex >}} - halocarboxylic acid**

{{< katex >}}\alpha{{< /katex >}} - chloroacetic acid when boiled with aqueous solution of sodium nitrite gives nitromethane.

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{Cl} - \mathrm{CH}_{2} - \mathrm{COOH} + \mathrm{NaNO}_{2}\xrightarrow[\mathrm{SN}^{2}]{\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} / \mathrm{Heat}}\mathrm{CH}_{3} - \mathrm{NO}_{2} + \mathrm{CO}_{2} + \mathrm{NaCl}{{< /katex >}}

#### 13.1.7 Physical properties of nitroalkanes

The lower nitroalkanes are colourless pleasant smelling liquids, sparingly soluble in water, but readily soluble in organic solvents like benzene, acetone etc. They have high boiling points because of their highly polar nature. Alkyl nitrites have lower boiling points than nitro alkanes.

**4) From alkyl halides**

**a) Gabriel phthalimide synthesis**

Gabriel synthesis is used for the preparation of aliphatic primary amines. Phthalimide on treatment with ethanolic KOH forms potassium salt of phthalimide which on heating with alkyl halide followed by alkaline hydrolysis gives primary amine. Aniline cannot be prepared by this method because the aryl halides do not undergo nucleophilic substitution with the anion formed by phthalimide.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Gabriel phthalimide synthesis</center>

**b) Hoffmann's ammonolysis**

When alkyl halides (or) benzyl halides are heated with alcoholic ammonia in a sealed tube, mixtures of {{< katex >}}1^{0}{{< /katex >}}, {{< katex >}}2^{0}{{< /katex >}} and {{< katex >}}3^{0}{{< /katex >}} amines and quaternary ammonium salts are obtained.

This is a nucleophilic substitution, the halide ion of alkyl halide is substituted by the - {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NH}_2{{< /katex >}} group. The product primary amine so formed can also act as a nucleophile and hence if excess alkyl halide is taken, further nucleophilic substitution takes place leading to secondary, tertiary and quaternary salts.

Lower aliphatic amines are soluble in water, because they can form hydrogen bonds with water molecules. **Influence of structure on basic character of amines**

The factors which increase the availability of electron pair on nitrogen for sharing with an acid will increase the basic character of an amine. When a {{< katex >}}+1{{< /katex >}} group like an alkyl group is attached to the nitrogen, it increases the electron density on nitrogen which makes the electron pair readily available for protonation.

a) Hence alkyl amines are stronger bases than ammonia.

Consider the reaction of an alkyl amine (R-NH2) with a proton.

The electron-releasing alkyl group R pushes electrons towards nitrogen in the amine {{< katex >}}\mathrm{(R - NH_2)}{{< /katex >}} and makes the unshared electron pair more available for sharing with proton.

Therefore, the expected order of basicity of aliphatic amines (in gas phase) is

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{R_3N > R_2NH > RNH_2 > NH_3}{{< /katex >}}

The above order is not regular in their aqueous solution as evident by their pKb values given in the table.

To compare the basicity of amines, the inductive effect, solvation effect, steric hindrance, etc., should be taken into consideration.

**Solvation effect**

In aqueous solution, the substituted ammonium cations get stabilized not only by electron releasing ({{< katex >}}+1{{< /katex >}}) effect of the alkyl group but also by solvation with water molecules. The greater the size of the ion, lesser will be the solvation. The order of stability of the protonated amines is: greater the size of the ion, lesser is the solvation and lesser is the stability. In case of secondary and tertiary amines, due to steric hindrance, the alkyl groups decrease the number of water molecules that can approach the protonated amine.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Solvation effects on amine basicity</center>

Based on these effects we can conclude that the order of basic strength in case of alkyl substituted amines in aqueous solution is

{{< katex display=true >}}(\mathrm{CH}_3)_2\mathrm{NH} > \mathrm{CH}_3\mathrm{NH}_2 > (\mathrm{CH}_3)_3\mathrm{N} > \mathrm{NH}_3{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}(\mathrm{C}_2\mathrm{H}_5)_2\mathrm{NH} > (\mathrm{C}_2\mathrm{H}_5)_3\mathrm{N} > \mathrm{C}_2\mathrm{H}_5\mathrm{NH}_2 > \mathrm{NH}_3{{< /katex >}}

The resultant of {{< katex >}}+1{{< /katex >}} effect, steric effect and hydration effect cause the {{< katex >}}2^{0}{{< /katex >}} amine to be more basic.

**Basic strength of aniline**

In aniline, the {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NH}_2{{< /katex >}} group is directly attached to the benzene ring. The lone pair of electrons on nitrogen atom in aniline gets delocalised over the benzene ring and hence it is less available for protonation, making the aromatic amines (aniline) less basic than {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NH}_3{{< /katex >}}.

In case of substituted aniline, electron releasing groups like {{< katex >}}-\mathrm{CH}_3, -\mathrm{OCH}_3, -\mathrm{NH}_2{{< /katex >}} increase the basic strength and electron withdrawing groups like {{< katex >}}-\mathrm{NO}_2, -\mathrm{X}, -\mathrm{COOH}{{< /katex >}} decrease the basic strength.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Alkylation of amines</center>

**2) Acylation**

Aliphatic / aromatic primary and secondary amines react with acetyl chloride (or) acetic anhydride in presence of pyridine to form N-alkyl acetamide.

**Example**

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Acylation of amines</center>

**3) Schotten - Baumann reaction**

Aniline reacts with benzoyl chloride {{< katex >}}\mathrm{(C_6H_5COCl)}{{< /katex >}} in the presence of NaOH to give N-phenyl benzamide. This reaction is known as Schotten-Baumann reaction. The acylation and benzoylation are nucleophilic substitutions.

**4) Reaction with nitrous acid**

Three classes of amines react differently with nitrous acid which is prepared in situ from a mixture of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NaNO}_2{{< /katex >}} and HCl.

**a) Primary amines**

i) Ethylamine reacts with nitrous acid to give ethyl diazonium chloride, which is unstable and it is converted to ethanol by liberating {{< katex >}}\mathrm{N}_2{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{C_2H_5 - NH_2 + HNO_2\xrightarrow[\mathrm{Conc.\ HCl}]{\mathrm{NaNO_2}}[C_2H_5N_2Cl]\xrightarrow[\mathrm{Unstable}]{\mathrm{H_2O}}C_2H_5OH + N_2}{{< /katex >}}

ii) Aniline reacts with nitrous acid at low temperature (273-278K) to give benzene diazonium chloride which is stable for a short time and slowly decomposes even at low temperatures. This reaction is known as diazotization.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Diazotization of aniline</center>

**b) Secondary amines**

Alkyl and aryl secondary amines react with nitrous acid to give N-nitroso amine as yellow oily liquid which is insoluble in water.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Reaction of secondary amines with nitrous acid</center>

This reaction is known as Libermann's nitroso test.

**c) Tertiary amine**

i) Aliphatic tertiary amine reacts with nitrous acid to form trialkyl ammonium nitrite salt, which is soluble in water.

ii) Aromatic tertiary amine reacts with nitrous acid at 273K to give p-nitroso compound.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Reaction of aromatic tertiary amine with nitrous acid</center>

**5) Carbylamine reaction**

Aliphatic (or) aromatic primary amines react with chloroform and alcoholic KOH to give isocyanides (carbylamines), which have an unpleasant smell. This reaction is known as carbylamine test. This test is used to identify primary amines.

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{C_2H_5 - NH_2 + CHCl_3 + 3KOH\longrightarrow C_2H_5 - NC + 3KCl + 3H_2O}{{< /katex >}}

**6) Mustard oil reaction**

i) When primary amines are treated with carbon disulphide {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{CS}_2){{< /katex >}}, N-alkylthio carbamic acid is formed which on subsequent treatment with {{< katex >}}\mathrm{HgCl}_2{{< /katex >}} gives an alkyl isothiocyanate.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Mustard oil reaction with primary amines</center>

ii) When aniline is treated with carbon disulphide, or heated together, S-diphenylthio urea is formed, which on boiling with strong HCl gives phenyl isothiocyanate (phenyl mustard oil).

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Mustard oil reaction with aniline</center>

These reactions are known as Hofmann-Mustard oil reaction. This test is used to identify primary amines.

**7. Electrophilic substitution reactions in Aniline**

The {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NH}_{2}{{< /katex >}} group is a strong activating group. In aniline the {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NH}_{2}{{< /katex >}} group is directly attached to the benzene ring, the lone pair of electrons on the nitrogen is in conjugation with benzene ring which increases the electron density at ortho and para positions, thereby facilitating the electrophilic attack at ortho and para positions.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Resonance in aniline</center>

**i) Bromination**

Aniline reacts with {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Br}_2 / \mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O}{{< /katex >}} to give 2,4,6-tribromo aniline as a white precipitate.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Bromination of aniline</center>

To get mono bromo compounds, - {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NH}_2{{< /katex >}} is first acylated to reduce its activity.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Acylation before bromination</center>

When aniline is acylated, the lone pair of electrons on nitrogen is delocalised by the neighbouring carbonyl group by resonance. Hence it is not easily available for conjugation with benzene ring.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Resonance in acetanilide</center>

The acetylamino group is thus less activating than the amino group in electrophilic substitution reaction.

**ii) Nitration**

Direct nitration of aniline gives o and p-nitro aniline along with dark coloured 'tars' due to oxidation. Moreover in a strong acid medium aniline is protonated to form anilinium ion which is m-directing and hence m-nitro aniline is also formed.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Nitration of aniline</center>

To get para product, the - {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NH}_2{{< /katex >}} group is protected by acetylation with acetic anhydride. Then, the nitrated product is hydrolysed to form the product.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Protection of aniline for nitration</center>

**iii) Sulphonation**

Aniline reacts with conc. {{< katex >}}\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}{{< /katex >}} to form anilinium hydrogen sulphate which on heating with {{< katex >}}\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}{{< /katex >}} at 453-473K gives p-aminobenzene sulphonic acid, commonly known as sulphanilic acid, as the major product.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Sulphonation of aniline</center>

**iv) Aniline does not undergo Friedel-Crafts reaction** (alkylation and acetylation) because aniline is basic in nature and it donates its lone pair to the Lewis acid {{< katex >}}\mathrm{AlCl}_3{{< /katex >}} to form an adduct which inhibits the electrophilic substitution reaction.

## 13.3 DIAZONIUM SALTS

### 13.3.1 Introduction

We have just learnt that aromatic amines on treatment with {{< katex >}}\mathrm{(NaNO_2 + HCl)}{{< /katex >}} gives diazonium salts. They are stable only for a short time and hence are used immediately after preparation.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Diazonium salt formation</center>

### 13.3.2 Resonance structure

The stability of arene diazonium salt is due to the dispersal of the positive charge over the benzene ring.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Resonance structures of diazonium salt</center>

### 13.3.3 Method of preparation of Diazonium salts

We have already learnt that benzene diazonium chloride is prepared by the reaction of aniline with nitrous acid (which is produced by the reaction of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NaNO}_2{{< /katex >}} and HCl) at 273-278K.

### 13.3.4 Physical properties

- Benzene diazonium chloride is a colourless, crystalline solid.
- These are readily soluble in water and stable in cold water. However it reacts with warm water.
- Their aqueous solutions are neutral to litmus and conduct electricity due to the presence of ions.
- Benzenediazonium tetrafluoroborate is soluble in water and stable at room temperature.

### 13.3.5 Chemical reactions

Benzene diazonium chloride gives two types of chemical reactions:

A. Replacement reactions involving loss of nitrogen (diazonium group is replaced by nucleophiles such as X, CN, H, OH, etc.)
B. Reactions involving retention of diazo group (Coupling reactions)

**1. Replacement by hydrogen**

Benzene diazonium chloride on reduction with mild reducing agents like hypophosphorous acid (phosphinic acid) or ethanol in the presence of cuprous ion gives benzene. This reaction proceeds through a free-radical chain mechanism.

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{C_6H_5N_2Cl + H_3PO_2 + H_2O\xrightarrow{CuCl} C_6H_6 + H_3PO_3 + HCl + N_2\uparrow}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{C_6H_5N_2Cl + CH_3CH_2OH\xrightarrow{} C_6H_6 + N_2 + CH_3CHO + HCl}{{< /katex >}}

**2. Replacement by Chlorine, Bromine, Cyanide group**

**a) Sandmeyer reaction**

On mixing freshly prepared solution of benzene diazonium chloride with cuprous halides (chlorides and bromides), aryl halides are obtained. This reaction is called Sandmeyer reaction.

When diazonium salts are treated with cuprous cyanide, cyanobenzene is obtained.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Sandmeyer reaction</center>

**b) Gattermann reaction**

Conversion of benzene diazonium chloride into chloro/bromo arenes can also be effected using hydrochloric/hydrobromic acid and copper powder. This reaction is called Gattermann reaction.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Gattermann reaction</center>

The yield in Sandmeyer reaction is found to be better than the Gattermann reaction.

**3. Replacement by iodine**

Aqueous solution of benzene diazonium chloride is warmed with KI to form iodobenzene.

**4. Replacement of fluorine (Balz-Schiemann reaction)**

When benzene diazonium chloride is treated with fluoroboric acid, benzene diazonium tetrafluoroborate is precipitated which on heating decomposes to give fluorobenzene.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Balz-Schiemann reaction</center>

**5. Replacement by hydroxyl group**

Benzene diazonium chloride solution is added slowly to a large volume of boiling water to get phenol.

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{C_6H_5 - N_2Cl + H_2O\xrightarrow[\Delta]{283K}C_6H_5 - OH + N_2 + HCl}{{< /katex >}}

**6. Replacement by nitro group**

When diazonium fluoroborate is heated with aqueous sodium nitrite solution in the presence of copper, the diazonium group is replaced by - {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NO}_{2}{{< /katex >}} group.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Replacement by nitro group</center>

**7. Replacement by aryl group (Gomberg reaction)**

Benzene diazonium chloride reacts with benzene in the presence of sodium hydroxide to give biphenyl. This reaction is known as the Gomberg reaction.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Gomberg reaction</center>

**8. Replacement by carboxylic acid group**

When diazonium fluoroborate is heated with acetic acid, benzoic acid is obtained. This reaction is used to convert aliphatic carboxylic acid into aromatic carboxylic acid.

**9. Reduction to hydrazines**

Certain reducing agents like {{< katex >}}\mathrm{SnCl}_2 / \mathrm{HCl}{{< /katex >}}; Zn dust / {{< katex >}}\mathrm{CH}_3\mathrm{COOH}{{< /katex >}}, sodium hydrosulphite, sodium sulphite etc. reduce benzene diazonium chloride to phenyl hydrazine.

**10. Coupling reactions**

Benzene diazonium chloride reacts with electron rich aromatic compounds like phenol, aniline to form brightly coloured azo compounds. Coupling generally occurs at the para position. If para position is occupied then coupling occurs at the ortho position. Coupling tendency is enhanced if an electron donating group is present at the para-position to {{< katex >}}\mathrm{-N}_2^+{{< /katex >}} group. This is an electrophilic substitution.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<center>Figure: Coupling reactions of diazonium salts</center>

Aryl fluorides and iodides cannot be prepared by direct halogenation and the cyano group cannot be introduced by nucleophilic substitution of chlorine in chlorobenzene. For introducing such halide groups, cyano group - OH, {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NO}_2{{< /katex >}} etc., benzenediazonium chloride is a very good intermediate. Diazo compounds obtained from the coupling reactions of diazonium salts are coloured and are used as dyes.

## 13.4 CYANIDES AND ISOCYANIDES

### 13.4.1 Introduction

These are the derivatives of hydrocyanic acid (HCN), which is known to exist in two tautomeric forms:

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{H-C\equiv N \rightleftharpoons H-N\equiv C}{{< /katex >}}

Two types of alkyl derivatives can be obtained. Those derived by replacement of H-atom of hydrogen cyanide by the alkyl groups are known as alkyl cyanides (R-C≡N), and those obtained by the replacement of H-atom of hydrogen isocyanide are known as alkyl isocyanides (R-N≡C).

In IUPAC system, alkyl cyanides are named as "alkanenitriles" whereas aryl cyanides as "arene carbonitriles".

### 13.4.2 Methods of preparation of cyanides

**1) From alkyl halides**

When alkyl halides are treated with solution of NaCN (or) KCN, alkyl cyanides are obtained. In this reaction a new carbon-carbon bond is formed.

**Example**

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{KCN + CH_3CH_2Br\longrightarrow CH_3CH_2CN + KBr}{{< /katex >}}

Aryl cyanide cannot be prepared by this method because of their low reactivity towards nucleophilic substitution. Aryl cyanides are prepared using Sandmeyer reactions.

**2. By dehydration of primary amides and aldoximes with {{< katex >}}\mathrm{P}_2\mathrm{O}_5{{< /katex >}}**

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{CH_3CONH_2\xrightarrow[\mathrm{-H_2O}]{\mathrm{P_2O_5}}CH_3CN}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{CH_3CH=NOH\xrightarrow[\mathrm{-H_2O}]{\mathrm{P_2O_5}}CH_3CN}{{< /katex >}}

**3. By dehydration of ammonium carboxylates with {{< katex >}}\mathrm{P}_2\mathrm{O}_5{{< /katex >}}**

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{CH_3COONH_4\xrightarrow[\Delta]{\mathrm{P_2O_5}}CH_3CN + 2H_2O}{{< /katex >}}

This method is suitable for large scale preparation of alkyl cyanides.

**4. From Grignard reagent**

Methyl magnesium bromide on treatment with cyanogen chloride (Cl-CN) forms ethanenitrile.

### 13.4.3 Properties of Cyanides

**Physical Properties**

The lower members (up to {{< katex >}}\mathrm{C}_{14}{{< /katex >}}) are colourless liquids with a strong characteristic sweet smell. The higher members are crystalline solids. They are moderately soluble in water but freely soluble in organic solvents. They are poisonous.

They have higher boiling points than analogous acetylenes due to their high dipole moments.

**1. Hydrolysis**

On boiling with alkali (or) a dilute mineral acid, the cyanides are hydrolysed to give carboxylic acids.

On reduction with {{< katex >}}\mathrm{LiAlH_4}{{< /katex >}} (or) {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Ni / H_2}{{< /katex >}}, alkyl cyanides yield primary amines.

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{CH_3 - CN + 2H_2\xrightarrow{Ni}CH_3 - CH_2 - NH_2}{{< /katex >}}

**3. Condensation reactions**

**a) Thorpe nitrile condensation**

Self condensation of two molecules of alkyl nitrile (containing {{< katex >}}\alpha - \mathrm{H}{{< /katex >}} atom) in the presence of sodium to form iminonitrile.

The nitriles containing {{< katex >}}\alpha{{< /katex >}} - hydrogen also undergo condensation with esters in the presence of sodamide in ether to form ketonitriles. This reaction is known as "Levine and Hauser" acetylation.

This reaction involves replacement of ethoxy {{< katex >}}\mathrm{(OC_2H_5)}{{< /katex >}} group by methylnitrile (-CH2CN) group and is called as cyanomethylation reaction.

### 13.4.7 Properties of isocyanides

**Physical properties**

- They are colourless, highly unpleasant smelling volatile liquids and are much more poisonous than the cyanides.
- They are only slightly soluble in water but are soluble in organic solvents.
- They are relatively less polar than alkyl cyanides. Thus, their melting point and boiling point are lower than cyanides.

### 13.4.8 Chemical properties

**1) Hydrolysis:** Alkyl isocyanides are not hydrolysed by alkalies. However they are hydrolysed with dilute mineral acids to give primary amines and formic acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{CH_3 - NC + 2H_2O\xrightarrow{acid}CH_3 - NH_2 + HCOOH}{{< /katex >}}

**2. Reduction:** When reduced catalytically (or) by nascent hydrogen, they give secondary amines.

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{CH_3 - NC + 4[H]}\xrightarrow{\mathrm{Na / C_2H_5OH\ (or)\ Ni / H_2}}\mathrm{CH_3 - NH - CH_3}{{< /katex >}}

**3. Isomerisation:** When alkyl isocyanides are heated at 250°C, they change into the more stable, isomeric cyanides.

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{CH_3 - N\equiv C\xrightarrow{Heat}CH_3 - C\equiv N}{{< /katex >}}

**4. Addition reactions:** Alkyl isocyanides add on halogen, sulphur, and oxygen to form the corresponding addition compounds.

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{R - N\equiv C + X_2\longrightarrow R - N\equiv CX_2}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{R - N\equiv C + S\longrightarrow R - N\equiv C = S\ (Alkyl\ isothiocyanate)}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}\mathrm{R - N\equiv C + O\longrightarrow R - N\equiv C = O\ (Alkyl\ isocyanate)}{{< /katex >}}

### 13.4.9 Uses of organic nitrogen compounds

**Nitroalkanes**

1. Nitromethane is used as a fuel for cars.
2. Chloropicrin {{< katex >}}\mathrm{(CCl_3NO_2)}{{< /katex >}} is used as an insecticide.
3. Nitroethane is used as a fuel additive and precursor to explosives and they are good solvents for polymers, cellulose ester, synthetic rubber and dyes, etc.
4. 4% solution of ethyl nitrite in alcohol is known as sweet spirit of nitre and is used as diuretic.

**Nitrobenzene**

1. Nitrobenzene is used to produce lubricating oils in motors and machinery.
2. It is used in the manufacture of dyes, drugs, pesticides, synthetic rubber, aniline and explosives like TNT, TNB.

**Cyanides and Isocyanides**

1. Alkyl cyanides are important intermediates in the organic synthesis of a large number of compounds like acids, amides, esters, amines, etc.
2. Nitriles are used in textile industry in the manufacture of nitrile rubber and also as a solvent particularly in perfume industry.

**Cancer Drug**

Mitomycin C, an anticancer agent used to treat stomach and colon cancer, contains an aziridine ring. The aziridine functional group participates in the drug's degradation by DNA, resulting in the death of cancerous cells.
