# p-BLOCK ELEMENTS-I

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<!-- Figure: Kenneth Wade (1932-2014) -->

**Kenneth Wade (1932-2014)** was a British chemist, and professor emeritus at Durham University. He developed a method for the prediction of shapes of borane clusters. Wade's rules are used to rationalize the shape of borane clusters by calculating the total number of skeletal electron pairs (SEP) available for cluster bonding. ## Learning Objectives

After studying this unit, the students will be able to

- describe the general trends in the properties of p-block elements
- explain the anomalous properties of the first element of p-block groups
- discuss the preparation, properties and uses of boron
- discuss the preparation of important compounds of boron and aluminium
- discuss the preparation and properties of important compounds of carbon and silicon

We have already learnt the classification of elements into four blocks namely s, p, d and f. We have also learnt the properties of s-block elements and their important compounds in the XI standard. This year we learn the elements of remaining blocks, starting with p-block elements.

The elements in which their last electron enters the 'p' orbital, constitute the p-block elements. They are placed in {{< katex >}}13^{\mathrm{th}}{{< /katex >}} to {{< katex >}}18^{\mathrm{th}}{{< /katex >}} groups of the modern periodic table and the first member of the groups are B, C, N, O, F and He respectively. These elements have quite varied properties and this block contains nonmetals, metals and metalloids. Nonmetallic elements of this group have more varied properties than metals. The elements of this block and their compounds play an important role in our day to day life, for example, without molecular oxygen we cannot imagine the survival of living system. The most abundant metal aluminium and its alloys have plenty of applications ranging from household utensils to parts of aircraft. The semi conducting nature of elements such as silicon and germanium made a revolutionary change in the field of modern electronics. In this unit we discuss the properties of first three groups (Group 13 - 15) of p-block elements namely boron, carbon and nitrogen family elements and their important compounds.

### 2.1 General trends in properties of p-block elements:

We already learnt that the properties of elements largely depends on their electronic configuration, size, ionisation enthalpy, electronegativity etc. Let us discuss the general trend in such properties of various p-block elements.

#### 2.1.1 Electronic configuration and oxidation state:

The p-block elements have a general electronic configuration of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{ns}^2\ \mathrm{np}^{1-6}{{< /katex >}}. The elements of each group have similar outer shell electronic configuration and differ only in the value of n (principal quantum number). The elements of group 18 (inert gases) have completely filled p orbitals, hence they are more stable and have least reactivity. The elements of this block show variable oxidation state and their highest oxidation state (group oxidation state) is equal to the total number of valence electrons present in them. Unlike s-block elements which show only positive oxidation state, some of the p-block elements show negative oxidation states also. The halogens have a strong tendency to gain an electron to give a stable halide ion with completely filled electronic configuration and hence -1 oxidation state is more common in halogens. Similarly, the other elements belonging to pnictogen and chalcogen groups also show negative oxidation states.

#### 2.1.2 Metallic and non-metallic character

In p-block, the elements present in lower left part are metals while the elements in the upper right part are non metals. Elements of group 13 have metallic character except the first element boron which is a metalloid, having properties intermediate between the metal and nonmetals. The atomic radius of boron is very small and it has relatively high nuclear charge and these properties are responsible for its nonmetallic character. In the subsequent groups the non-metallic character increases. In group 14 elements, carbon is a nonmetal while silicon and germanium are metalloids. In group 15, nitrogen and phosphorus are non metals and arsenic & antimony are metalloids. In group 16, oxygen, sulphur and selenium are non metals and tellurium is a metalloid. All the elements of group 17 and 18 are non metals.

#### 2.1.3 Ionisation Enthalpy:

We have already learnt that as we move down a group, generally there is a steady decrease in ionisation enthalpy of elements due to increase in their atomic radius. In p-block elements, there are some minor deviations to this general trend. In group 13, from boron to aluminium the ionisation enthalpy decreases as expected. But from aluminium to thallium there is only a marginal difference. This is due to the presence of inner d and f-electrons which has poor shielding effect compared to s and p-electrons. As a result, the effective nuclear charge on the valence electrons increases. A similar trend is also observed in group 14. The remaining groups (15 to 18) follow the general trend. In these groups, the ionisation enthalpy decreases, as we move down the group. Here, poor shielding effect of d- and f-electrons are overcome by the increased shielding effect of the additional p-electrons. The ionisation enthalpy of elements in successive groups is higher than the corresponding elements of the previous group as expected.

#### 2.1.4 Electronegativity

As we move down the {{< katex >}}13^{\mathrm{th}}{{< /katex >}} group, the electronegativity first decreases from boron to aluminium and then marginally increases for Gallium, thereafter there is no appreciable change. Similar trend is also observed in {{< katex >}}14^{\mathrm{th}}{{< /katex >}} group as well. In other groups, as we move down the group, the electronegativity decreases. This observed trend can be correlated with their atomic radius.

#### 2.1.5 Anomalous properties of the first elements:

In p-block elements, the first member of each group differs from the other elements of the corresponding group. The following factors are responsible for this anomalous behaviour.

1. Small size of the first member
2. High ionisation enthalpy and high electronegativity
3. Absence of d orbitals in their valence shell

The first member of the group 13, boron is a metalloid while others are reactive metals. Moreover, boron shows diagonal relationship with silicon of group 14. The oxides of boron and silicon are similar in their acidic nature. Both boron and silicon form covalent hydrides that can be easily hydrolysed. Similarly, except boron trifluoride, halides of both elements are readily hydrolysed.

In group 14, the first element carbon is strictly a nonmetal while other elements are metalloids (silicon & germanium) or metals (tin & lead). Unlike other elements of the group carbon can form multiple bonds such as {{< katex >}}\mathrm{C = C}{{< /katex >}}, {{< katex >}}\mathrm{C = O}{{< /katex >}} etc. Carbon has a greater tendency to form a chain of bonds with itself or with other atoms which is known as catenation. There is considerable decrease in catenation property down the group ({{< katex >}}\mathrm{C} \gg \mathrm{Si} > \mathrm{Ge} = \mathrm{Sn} > \mathrm{Pb}{{< /katex >}}).

In group 15 also the first element nitrogen differs from the rest of the elements of the group. Like carbon, nitrogen can form multiple bonds {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{N = N, C = N, N = O}{{< /katex >}} etc.). Nitrogen is a diatomic gas unlike the other members of the group. Similarly in group 16, the first element, oxygen also exists as a diatomic gas in that group. Due to its high electronegativity it forms hydrogen bonds.

The first element of group 17, fluorine the most electronegative element, also behaves quite differently compared to the rest of the members of group. Like oxygen it also forms hydrogen bonds. It shows only -1 oxidation state while the other halogens have {{< katex >}}+1, +3, +5{{< /katex >}} and {{< katex >}}+7{{< /katex >}} oxidation states in addition to -1 state. Fluorine is the strongest oxidising agent and the most reactive element among the halogens.

#### 2.1.6 Inert pair effect:

We have already learnt that the alkali and alkaline earth metals have an oxidation state of {{< katex >}}+1{{< /katex >}} and {{< katex >}}+2{{< /katex >}} respectively, corresponding to the total number of electrons present in them. Similarly, the elements of p-block also show the oxidation states corresponding to the maximum number of valence electrons (group oxidation state). In addition they also show variable oxidation state. In case of the heavier post-transition elements belonging to the groups (13 to 16), the most stable oxidation state is two less than the group oxidation state and there is a reluctance to exhibit the group oxidation state. Let us consider group 13 elements. As we move from boron to heavier elements, there is an increasing tendency to have {{< katex >}}+1{{< /katex >}} oxidation state, rather than the group oxidation state, {{< katex >}}+3{{< /katex >}}. For example {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Al}^{+3}{{< /katex >}} is more stable than {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Al}^{+1}{{< /katex >}} while {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Tl}^{+1}{{< /katex >}} is more stable than {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Tl}^{+3}{{< /katex >}}. Aluminium(III) chloride is stable whereas thallium(III) chloride is highly unstable and disproportionates to thallium(I) chloride and chlorine gas. This shows that in thallium the stable lower oxidation state corresponds to the loss of np electrons only and not ns electrons. Thus in heavier post-transition metals, the outer s electrons (ns) have a tendency to remain inert and show reluctance to take part in the bonding, which is known as inert pair effect. This effect is also observed in groups 14, 15 and 16.

#### 2.1.7 Allotropism in p-block elements:

Some elements exist in more than one crystalline or molecular forms in the same physical state. For example, carbon exists as diamond and graphite. This phenomenon is called allotropism (in Greek 'allos' means another and 'trope' means change) and the different forms of an element are called allotropes. Many p-block elements show allotropism and some of the common allotropes are listed in the table.

### 2.2 Group 13 (Boron group) elements:

#### 2.2.1 Occurrence:

Boron occurs mostly as borates and its important ones are borax - {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Na}_{2}[\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{5}(\mathrm{OH})_{4}] \cdot 8\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}{{< /katex >}} and kernite - {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Na}_{2}[\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{5}(\mathrm{OH})_{4}] \cdot 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}{{< /katex >}}. Aluminium is the most abundant metal and occurs as oxides and also found in aluminosilicate rocks. Commercially it is extracted from its chief ore, bauxite ({{< katex >}}\mathrm{Al}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3} \cdot 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}{{< /katex >}}). The other elements of this group occur only in trace amounts. The other elements Ga, In and Tl occur as their sulphides.

#### 2.2.3 Chemical properties of boron:

Boron is the only nonmetal in this group and is less reactive. However, it shows reactivity at higher temperatures. Many of its compounds are electron deficient and have unusual type of covalent bonding which is due to its small size, high ionisation energy and similarity in electronegativity with carbon and hydrogen.

**Formation of metal borides:** Many metals except alkali metals form borides with a general formula {{< katex >}}\mathrm{M}_{x}\mathrm{B}_{y}{{< /katex >}} (x ranging up to 11 and y ranging up to 66 or higher).

Direct combination of metals with boron:

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Cr} + n\mathrm{B} \xrightarrow{1500\mathrm{K}} \mathrm{CrB}_{n}
{{< /katex >}}

Reduction of boron trihalides with a metal assisted by dihydrogen gives metal borides.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{BCl}_{3} + 2\mathrm{W} \xrightarrow{1500\mathrm{K}} \mathrm{H}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{WB} + 2\mathrm{Cl}_{2} + 2\mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}

**Formation of hydrides:** Boron does not react directly with hydrogen. However, it forms a variety of hydrides called boranes. The simplest borane is diborane - {{< katex >}}\mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{6}{{< /katex >}}. Other larger boranes can be prepared from diborane. Treatment of gaseous boron trifluoride with sodium hydride around 450 K gives diborane. To prevent subsequent pyrolysis, the product diborane is trapped immediately.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{BF}_{3} + 6\mathrm{NaH} \xrightarrow{450\mathrm{K}} \mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{6} + 6\mathrm{NaF}
{{< /katex >}}

**Formation of boron trihalides:** Boron combines with halogen to form boron trihalides at high temperatures.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{B} + 3\mathrm{X}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{BX}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

**Formation of boron nitride:** Boron burns with dinitrogen at high temperatures to form boron nitride.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{B} + \mathrm{N}_{2} \xrightarrow{\Delta} 2\mathrm{BN}
{{< /katex >}}

**Formation of oxides:** When boron is heated with oxygen around {{< katex >}}900\mathrm{K}{{< /katex >}}, it forms its oxide.

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{B} + 3\mathrm{O}_{2} \xrightarrow{900\mathrm{K}} 2\mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

**Reaction with acids and alkali:** Halo acids have no reaction with boron. However, boron reacts with oxidising acids such as sulphuric acid and nitric acids and forms boric acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{B} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} + 3\mathrm{SO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{B} + 3\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} + 3\mathrm{NO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

Boron reacts with fused sodium hydroxide and forms sodium borate.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{B} + 6\mathrm{NaOH} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{Na}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

**Uses of boron:**

1. Boron has the capacity to absorb neutrons. Hence, its isotope {{< katex >}}^{10}\mathrm{B}_{5}{{< /katex >}} is used as moderator in nuclear reactors.
2. Amorphous boron is used as a rocket fuel igniter.
3. Boron is essential for the cell walls of plants.
4. Compounds of boron have many applications. For example eye drops, antiseptics, washing powders etc., contain boric acid and borax. In the manufacture of Pyrex glass, boric oxide is used.

#### 2.2.4 Borax {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} \cdot 10\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}]{{< /katex >}}

**Preparation:** Borax is a sodium salt of tetraboric acid. It is obtained from colemanite ore by boiling its solution with sodium carbonate.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{Ca}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{6}\mathrm{O}_{11} + 3\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{CO}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \xrightarrow{\Delta} 3\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} + 3\mathrm{CaCO}_{3} + \mathrm{Ca(OH)}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

Borax is normally formulated as {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} \cdot 10\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}{{< /katex >}}. But it contains tetranuclear units {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{5}(\mathrm{OH})_{4}]^{2-}{{< /katex >}}. This form is known as prismatic form. Borax also exists in two other forms namely, jeweller's or octahedral borax ({{< katex >}}\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} \cdot 5\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}{{< /katex >}}) and borax glass ({{< katex >}}\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7}{{< /katex >}}).

**Properties:** Borax is basic in nature and its solution in hot water is alkaline as it dissociates into boric acid and sodium hydroxide.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} + 7\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} + 2\mathrm{NaOH}
{{< /katex >}}

On heating it forms a transparent borax bead.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} \cdot 10\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \xrightarrow{\Delta} \mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{NaBO}_{2} + \mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

Borax reacts with acids to form sparingly soluble boric acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} + 2\mathrm{HCl} + 5\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} + 2\mathrm{NaCl}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + 5\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} + \mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}
{{< /katex >}}

When treated with ammonium chloride it forms boron nitride.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} + 2\mathrm{NH}_{4}\mathrm{Cl} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{NaCl} + 2\mathrm{BN} + \mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3} + 4\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**Uses of Borax:**

1. Borax is used for the identification of coloured metal ions.
2. In the manufacture of optical and borosilicate glass, enamels and glazes for pottery.
3. It is also used as a flux in metallurgy and also acts as a preservative.

#### 2.2.5 Boric acid {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3}{{< /katex >}} or {{< katex >}}\mathrm{B}(\mathrm{OH})_{3}]{{< /katex >}}

**Preparation:** Boric acid can be extracted from borax and colemanite.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + 5\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} + \mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Ca}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{11} + 11\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + 4\mathrm{SO}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{HSO}_{3})_{2} + 6\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

**Properties:** Boric acid is a colourless transparent crystal. It is a very weak monobasic acid and it accepts hydroxyl ion rather than donating a proton.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{B}(\mathrm{OH})_{3} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{O}^{+} + [\mathrm{B}(\mathrm{OH})_{4}]^{-}
{{< /katex >}}

It reacts with sodium hydroxide to form sodium metaborate and sodium tetraborate.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} + \mathrm{NaOH} \longrightarrow \mathrm{NaBO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} + 2\mathrm{NaOH} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} + 7\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**Action of Heat:** Boric acid when heated at {{< katex >}}373\mathrm{K}{{< /katex >}} gives metaboric acid and at {{< katex >}}413\mathrm{K}{{< /katex >}}, it gives tetraboric acid. When heated to red hot, it gives boric anhydride which is a glassy mass.

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} \xrightarrow{373\mathrm{K}} 4\mathrm{HBO}_{2} + 4\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{HBO}_{2} \xrightarrow{413\mathrm{K}} \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} \xrightarrow{\text{Red hot}} 2\mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**Action of ammonia:** Fusion of urea with {{< katex >}}\mathrm{B}(\mathrm{OH})_{3}{{< /katex >}} in an atmosphere of ammonia at {{< katex >}}800-1200\mathrm{K}{{< /katex >}} gives boron nitride.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{B}(\mathrm{OH})_{3} + \mathrm{NH}_{3} \xrightarrow{\Delta} \mathrm{BN} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**Ethyl Borate test:** When boric acid or borate salt is heated with ethyl alcohol in presence of conc. sulphuric acid, an ester, triethylborate is formed. The vapour of this ester burns with a green edged flame and this reaction is used to identify the presence of borate.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} + 3\mathrm{C}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{5}\mathrm{OH} \xrightarrow{\text{Conc. } \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}} \mathrm{B}(\mathrm{OC}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{5})_{3} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

Note: The trialkyl borate on reaction with sodium hydride in tetrahydrofuran forms a coordination compound {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Na}[\mathrm{B}(\mathrm{H}(\mathrm{OR})_{3})]{{< /katex >}}, which acts as a powerful reducing agent.

**Formation of boron trifluoride:** Boric acid reacts with calcium fluoride in presence of conc. sulphuric acid and gives boron trifluoride.

{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{CaF}_{2} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + 2\mathrm{B}(\mathrm{OH})_{3} \longrightarrow 3\mathrm{CaSO}_{4} + 2\mathrm{BF}_{3} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

Boric acid when heated with soda ash gives borax.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{CO}_{3} + 4\mathrm{B}(\mathrm{OH})_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{7} + \mathrm{CO}_{2} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

#### 2.2.6 Diborane

**Preparation:** As discussed earlier diborane can be prepared by the action of metal hydride with boron. This method is used for industrial production.

Diborane can also be obtained in small quantities by the reaction of iodine with sodium borohydride in diglyme.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{NaBH}_{4} + \mathrm{I}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{6} + 2\mathrm{NaI} + \mathrm{H}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

On heating magnesium boride with HCl a mixture of volatile boranes is obtained.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{Mg}_{3}\mathrm{B}_{2} + 12\mathrm{HCl} \longrightarrow 6\mathrm{MgCl}_{2} + \mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{H}_{10} + \mathrm{H}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{B}_{4}\mathrm{H}_{10} + \mathrm{H}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{6}
{{< /katex >}}

**Properties:** Boranes are colourless diamagnetic compounds with low thermal stability. Diborane is a gas at room temperature with sweet smell and it is extremely toxic. It is also highly reactive.

At high temperatures it forms higher boranes liberating hydrogen.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<!-- Figure: Diborane reaction -->

Diborane reacts with water and alkali to give boric acid and metaborates respectively.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{6} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{6} + 2\mathrm{NaOH} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{NaBO}_{2} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

**Action of air:** At room temperature pure diborane does not react with air or oxygen but in impure form it gives {{< katex >}}\mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3}{{< /katex >}} along with large amount of heat.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{6} + 3\mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \quad \Delta H = -2165\ \mathrm{kJ}\ \mathrm{mol}^{-1}
{{< /katex >}}

Diborane reacts with methyl alcohol to give trimethyl borate.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{6} + 6\mathrm{CH}_{3}\mathrm{OH} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{B}(\mathrm{OCH}_{3})_{3} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

**Hydroboration:** Diborane adds on to alkenes and alkynes in ether solvent at room temperature. This reaction is called hydroboration and is highly used in synthetic organic chemistry, especially for anti-Markovnikov addition.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{6} + 6\mathrm{RCH} = \mathrm{CHR} \longrightarrow 2(\mathrm{RCH}_{2}-\mathrm{CHR})_{3}\mathrm{B}
{{< /katex >}}

**Reaction with ionic hydrides:** When treated with metal hydrides it forms metal borohydrides.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{6} + 2\mathrm{LiH} \xrightarrow{\mathrm{Ether}} 2\mathrm{LiBH}_{4}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{6} + 2\mathrm{NaH} \xrightarrow{\mathrm{Diglyme}} 2\mathrm{NaBH}_{4}
{{< /katex >}}

**Reaction with ammonia:** When treated with excess ammonia at low temperatures diborane gives diborane diamminate. On heating at higher temperatures it gives borazole.

<!-- Figure: Reaction of diborane with ammonia -->

**Structure of diborane:** In diborane two {{< katex >}}\mathrm{BH}_{2}{{< /katex >}} units are linked by two bridged hydrogens. Therefore, it has eight B-H bonds. However, diborane has only 12 valence electrons which are not sufficient to form normal covalent bonds. The four terminal B-H bonds are normal covalent bonds (two centre - two electron bond or 2c-2e bond). The remaining four electrons have to be used for the bridged bonds, i.e., two three-centred B-H-B bonds utilise two electrons each. Hence, these bonds are three centre - two electron bonds (3c-2e). The bridging hydrogen atoms are in a plane as shown in Figure 2.3. In diborane, the boron is {{< katex >}}\mathrm{sp}^{3}{{< /katex >}} hybridised.

1. Diborane is used as a high energy fuel for propellants.
2. It is used as a reducing agent in organic chemistry.
3. It is used in welding torches.

#### 2.2.7 Boron trifluoride

**Preparation:** Boron trifluoride is obtained by the treatment of calcium fluoride with boron trioxide in presence of conc. sulphuric acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3} + 3\mathrm{CaF}_{2} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \xrightarrow{\Delta} 2\mathrm{BF}_{3} + 3\mathrm{CaSO}_{4} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

It can also be obtained by treating boron trioxide with carbon and fluorine.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{B}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3} + 3\mathrm{C} + 3\mathrm{F}_{2} \xrightarrow{\Delta} 2\mathrm{BF}_{3} + 3\mathrm{CO}
{{< /katex >}}

In the laboratory pure {{< katex >}}\mathrm{BF}_{3}{{< /katex >}} is prepared by the thermal decomposition of benzene diazonium tetrafluoroborate.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{C}_{6}\mathrm{H}_{5}\mathrm{N}_{2}\mathrm{BF}_{4} \xrightarrow{\Delta} \mathrm{BF}_{3} + \mathrm{C}_{6}\mathrm{H}_{5}\mathrm{F} + \mathrm{N}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

**Properties:** Boron trifluoride has a planar geometry. It is an electron deficient compound and accepts electron pairs to form coordinate covalent bonds. They form complexes of the type {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{BX}_{4}]^{-}{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{BF}_{3} + \mathrm{NH}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{F}_{3}\mathrm{B}-\mathrm{NH}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{BF}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{F}_{3}\mathrm{B}-\mathrm{OH}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

On hydrolysis, boric acid is obtained. This then gets converted into hydrofluoroboric acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{BF}_{3} + 12\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \xrightarrow{\Delta} 4\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} + 12\mathrm{HF}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} + 12\mathrm{HF} \xrightarrow{\Delta} 3\mathrm{H}^{+} + 3[\mathrm{BF}_{4}]^{-} + 9\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{BF}_{3} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \xrightarrow{\Delta} \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{BO}_{3} + 3\mathrm{H}^{+} + 3[\mathrm{BF}_{4}]^{-}
{{< /katex >}}

**Uses of Boron trifluoride:**

1. Boron trifluoride is used for preparing {{< katex >}}\mathrm{HBF}_{4}{{< /katex >}}, a catalyst in organic chemistry.
2. It is also used as a fluorinating reagent.

#### 2.2.8 Aluminium chloride

**Preparation:** When aluminium metal or aluminium hydroxide is treated with hydrochloric acid, aluminium trichloride is formed. The reaction mixture is evaporated to obtain hydrated aluminium chloride.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{Al} + 6\mathrm{HCl} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{AlCl}_{3} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Al}(\mathrm{OH})_{3} + 3\mathrm{HCl} \longrightarrow \mathrm{AlCl}_{3} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**McAfee Process:** Aluminium chloride is obtained by heating a mixture of alumina and coke in a current of chlorine.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{Al}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3} + 3\mathrm{C} + 6\mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{AlCl}_{3} + 3\mathrm{CO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

On industrial scale it is prepared by chlorinating aluminium around 1000 K.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{Al} + 3\mathrm{Cl}_{2} \xrightarrow{1000\mathrm{K}} 2\mathrm{AlCl}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

**Properties:** Anhydrous aluminium chloride is a colourless, hygroscopic substance.

An aqueous solution of aluminium chloride is acidic in nature. It also produces hydrogen chloride fumes in moist air.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{AlCl}_{3} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Al}(\mathrm{OH})_{3} + 3\mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}

With ammonium hydroxide it forms aluminium hydroxide.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{AlCl}_{3} + 3\mathrm{NH}_{4}\mathrm{OH} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Al}(\mathrm{OH})_{3} + 3\mathrm{NH}_{4}\mathrm{Cl}
{{< /katex >}}

With excess of sodium hydroxide it produces metal aluminate.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{AlCl}_{3} + 4\mathrm{NaOH} \longrightarrow \mathrm{NaAlO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + 3\mathrm{NaCl}
{{< /katex >}}

It behaves like a Lewis acid and forms addition compounds with ammonia, phosphine and carbonyl chloride etc. e.g., {{< katex >}}\mathrm{AlCl}_{3} \cdot 6\mathrm{NH}_{3}{{< /katex >}}.

**Uses of aluminium chloride:**

1. Anhydrous aluminium chloride is used as a catalyst in Friedel-Crafts reaction.
2. It is used for the manufacture of petrol by cracking mineral oils.
3. It is used as a catalyst in the manufacture of dyes, drugs and perfumes.

#### 2.2.9 Alums

The name alum is given to the double salt of potassium aluminium sulphate {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} \cdot 24\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}]{{< /katex >}}. Nowadays it is used for all the double salts with {{< katex >}}\mathrm{M}_{2}^{\prime}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{M}_{2}^{\prime\prime}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} \cdot 24\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}{{< /katex >}}, where {{< katex >}}\mathrm{M}^{\prime}{{< /katex >}} is a univalent metal ion or {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{NH}_{4}]^{+}{{< /katex >}} and {{< katex >}}\mathrm{M}^{\prime\prime}{{< /katex >}} is a trivalent metal ion.

**Examples:** Potash alum {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} \cdot 24\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}]{{< /katex >}}; Sodium alum {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} \cdot 24\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}]{{< /katex >}}; Ammonium alum {{< katex >}}[(\mathrm{NH}_{4})_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} \cdot 24\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}]{{< /katex >}}; Chrome alum {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{Cr}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} \cdot 24\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}]{{< /katex >}}.

Alums in general are more soluble in hot water than in cold water and in solutions they exhibit the properties of constituent ions.

**Preparation:** Alumine, the alum stone, is the naturally occurring form and it is {{< katex >}}\mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} \cdot 4\mathrm{Al}(\mathrm{OH})_{3}{{< /katex >}}. When alum stone is treated with excess of sulphuric acid, the aluminium hydroxide is converted to aluminium sulphate. A calculated quantity of potassium sulphate is added and the solution is crystallised to generate potash alum. It is purified by recrystallisation.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} \cdot 4\mathrm{Al}(\mathrm{OH})_{3} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + 3\mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} + 12\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + \mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} + 24\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} \cdot 24\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**Properties:** Potash alum is a white crystalline solid, soluble in water and insoluble in alcohol. The aqueous solution is acidic due to the hydrolysis of aluminium sulphate. It melts at 365 K. On heating at 475 K it loses water of hydration and swells up. The swollen mass is known as burnt alum. Heating to red hot it decomposes into potassium sulphate, alumina and sulphur trioxide.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} \cdot 24\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} + 24\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} \xrightarrow{\text{Red hot}} \mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + \mathrm{Al}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3} + 3\mathrm{SO}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

Potash alum forms aluminium hydroxide when treated with ammonium hydroxide.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} \cdot 24\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + 6\mathrm{NH}_{4}\mathrm{OH} \longrightarrow \mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + 3(\mathrm{NH}_{4})_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + 24\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + 2\mathrm{Al}(\mathrm{OH})_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

**Uses of Alum:**

1. It is used for purification of water.
2. It is also used for waterproofing textiles.
3. It is used in dyeing, paper and leather tanning industries.
4. It is employed as a styptic agent to arrest bleeding.

#### 2.3.3 Tendency for catenation

Catenation is the ability of an element to form chains of atoms. The following conditions are necessary for catenation: (i) valency of the element should be greater than or equal to two, (ii) element should have the ability to bond with itself, (iii) the self bond must be as strong as its bond with other elements, (iv) kinetic inertness of catenated compound towards other molecules. Carbon possesses all the above properties and forms a wide range of compounds with itself and with other elements such as H, O, N, S and halogens.

#### 2.3.4 Allotropes of carbon

Carbon exists in many allotropic forms. Graphite and diamond are the most common allotropes. Other important allotropes are graphene, fullerenes and carbon nanotubes.

Graphite is the most stable allotropic form of carbon at normal temperature and pressure. It is soft and conducts electricity. It is composed of flat two-dimensional sheets of carbon atoms. Each sheet is a hexagonal net of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{sp}^{2}{{< /katex >}} hybridised carbon atoms with a C-C bond length of {{< katex >}}1.41\ \mathrm{\AA}{{< /katex >}} which is close to the C-C bond distance in benzene (1.40 Å). Each carbon atom forms three {{< katex >}}\sigma{{< /katex >}} bonds with three neighbouring carbon atoms using three of its valence electrons and the fourth electron present in the unhybridised p orbital forms a {{< katex >}}\pi{{< /katex >}}-bond. These {{< katex >}}\pi{{< /katex >}} electrons are delocalised over the entire sheet which is responsible for its electrical conductivity. The successive carbon sheets are held together by weak van der Waals forces. The distance between successive sheets is 3.40 Å. It is used as a lubricant either on its own or as a graphited oil.

Unlike graphite the other allotrope diamond is very hard. The carbon atoms in diamond are {{< katex >}}\mathrm{sp}^{3}{{< /katex >}} hybridised and bonded to four neighbouring carbon atoms by {{< katex >}}\sigma{{< /katex >}} bonds with a C-C bond length of 1.54 Å. This results in a tetrahedral arrangement around each carbon atom that extends to the entire lattice as shown in Figure 2.5. Since all four valence electrons of carbon are involved in bonding there are no free electrons for conductivity. Being the hardest element, it is used for sharpening hard tools, cutting glasses, making bores and rock drilling.

Carbon nanotubes, another recently discovered allotrope, have graphite-like tubes with fullerene ends. Along the axis, these nanotubes are stronger than steel and conduct electricity. These have many applications in nanoscale electronics, catalysis, polymers and medicine.

#### 2.3.5 Carbon monoxide [CO]

**Preparation:** Carbon monoxide can be prepared by the reaction of carbon with a limited amount of oxygen.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{C} + \mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{CO}
{{< /katex >}}

On an industrial scale carbon monoxide is produced by the reaction of carbon with air. The carbon monoxide formed will contain nitrogen gas also and the mixture of nitrogen and carbon monoxide is called producer gas.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{C} + \mathrm{O}_{2}/\mathrm{N}_{2}\ (\text{air}) \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{CO} + \mathrm{N}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

The producer gas is then passed through a solution of copper(I) chloride under pressure which results in the formation of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{CuCl(CO) \cdot 2H_{2}O}{{< /katex >}}. At reduced pressure this solution releases the pure carbon monoxide.

Pure carbon monoxide is prepared by warming methanoic acid with concentrated sulphuric acid which acts as a dehydrating agent.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HCOOH} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CO} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**Properties:** It is a colourless, odourless, and poisonous gas. It is slightly soluble in water.

It burns in air with a blue flame forming carbon dioxide.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{CO} + \mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{CO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

When carbon monoxide is treated with chlorine in presence of light or charcoal, it forms a poisonous gas carbonyl chloride, which is also known as phosgene. It is used in the synthesis of isocyanates.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{CO} + \mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{COCl}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

Carbon monoxide acts as a strong reducing agent.

{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{CO} + \mathrm{Fe}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{Fe} + 3\mathrm{CO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

Under high temperature and pressure a mixture of carbon monoxide and hydrogen (synthetic gas or syngas) gives methanol.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{CO} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CH}_{3}\mathrm{OH}
{{< /katex >}}

In the oxo process, ethene is mixed with carbon monoxide and hydrogen gas to produce propanal.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{CO} + \mathrm{C}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{4} + \mathrm{H}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CH}_{3}\mathrm{CH}_{2}\mathrm{CHO}
{{< /katex >}}

**Fischer-Tropsch synthesis:** The reaction of carbon monoxide with hydrogen at a pressure of less than 50 atm using metal catalysts at {{< katex >}}500-700\ \mathrm{K}{{< /katex >}} yields saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbons.

{{< katex display=true >}}
n\mathrm{CO} + (2n+1)\mathrm{H}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{C}_{n}\mathrm{H}_{(2n+2)} + n\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
n\mathrm{CO} + 2n\mathrm{H}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{C}_{n}\mathrm{H}_{2n} + n\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

Carbon monoxide forms numerous complex compounds with transition metals in which the transition metal is in zero oxidation state. These compounds are obtained by heating the metal with carbon monoxide. E.g., Nickel tetracarbonyl {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{Ni}(\mathrm{CO})_{4}]{{< /katex >}}, Iron pentacarbonyl {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{Fe}(\mathrm{CO})_{5}]{{< /katex >}}, Chromium hexacarbonyl {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{Cr}(\mathrm{CO})_{6}]{{< /katex >}}.

**Structure:** It has a linear structure. In carbon monoxide, three electron pairs are shared between carbon and oxygen. The bonding can be explained using molecular orbital theory as discussed in XI standard. The C-O bond distance is {{< katex >}}1.128\ \mathrm{\AA}{{< /katex >}}. The structure can be considered as the resonance hybrid of the following two canonical forms.

**Uses of carbon monoxide:**

1. An equimolar mixture of hydrogen and carbon monoxide - water gas, and the mixture of carbon monoxide and nitrogen - producer gas are important industrial fuels.
2. Carbon monoxide is a good reducing agent and can reduce many metal oxides to metals.
3. Carbon monoxide is an important ligand and forms carbonyl compounds with transition metals.

#### 2.3.6 Carbon dioxide

Carbon dioxide occurs in nature in the free state as well as in the combined state. It is a constituent of air (0.03%). It occurs in rocks as calcium carbonate and magnesium carbonate.

**Production:** On an industrial scale it is produced by burning coke in excess of air.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{C} + \mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CO}_{2} \quad \Delta H = -394\ \mathrm{kJ}\ \mathrm{mol}^{-1}
{{< /katex >}}

Calcination of lime produces carbon dioxide as a by-product.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{CaCO}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CaO} + \mathrm{CO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

Carbon dioxide is prepared in the laboratory by the action of dilute hydrochloric acid on metal carbonates.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{CaCO}_{3} + 2\mathrm{HCl} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CaCl}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{CO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

**Properties:** It is a colourless, nonflammable gas and is heavier than air. Its critical temperature is {{< katex >}}31^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}} and can be readily liquefied.

Carbon dioxide is a very stable compound. Even at 3100 K only 76% decomposes to form carbon monoxide and oxygen. At still higher temperature it decomposes into carbon and oxygen.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{CO}_{2} \xrightarrow{3100\mathrm{K}} \mathrm{CO} + \frac{1}{2}\mathrm{O}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{CO}_{2} \xrightarrow{\text{high temperature}} \mathrm{CO} + \mathrm{O}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

**Oxidising behaviour:** At elevated temperatures, it acts as a strong oxidising agent. For example,

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{CO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{Mg} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{MgO} + \mathrm{C}
{{< /katex >}}

**Water gas equilibrium:** The equilibrium involved in the reaction between carbon dioxide and hydrogen has many industrial applications and is called the water gas equilibrium.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{CO}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{CO} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**Acidic behaviour:** The aqueous solution of carbon dioxide is slightly acidic as it forms carbonic acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{CO}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{CO}_{3} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}^{+} + \mathrm{HCO}_{3}^{-}
{{< /katex >}}

**Structure of carbon dioxide:** Carbon dioxide has a linear structure with equal bond distance for both C-O bonds. In this molecule there are two C-O sigma bonds. In addition there is a 3c-4e bond covering all three atoms.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<!-- Figure 2.10 Structure of carbon dioxide -->

**Uses of carbon dioxide:**

1. Carbon dioxide is used to produce an inert atmosphere for chemical processing.
2. Biologically, it is important for photosynthesis.
3. It is also used as a fire extinguisher and as a propellant gas.
4. It is used in the production of carbonated beverages and in the production of foam.

#### 2.3.7 Silicon tetrachloride

**Preparation:** Silicon tetrachloride can be prepared by passing dry chlorine over an intimate mixture of silica and carbon and heating to 1675 K in a porcelain tube.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{SiO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{C} + 2\mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{SiCl}_{4} + 2\mathrm{CO}
{{< /katex >}}

On a commercial scale, the reaction of silicon with hydrogen chloride gas occurs above 600 K.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Si} + 4\mathrm{HCl} \longrightarrow \mathrm{SiCl}_{4} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

**Properties:** Silicon tetrachloride is a colourless fuming liquid and it freezes at {{< katex >}}-70^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}}.

In moist air, silicon tetrachloride is hydrolysed with water to give silica and hydrochloric acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{SiCl}_{4} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{HCl} + \mathrm{SiO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

When silicon tetrachloride is hydrolysed with moist ether, linear perchlorosiloxanes are formed {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{Cl} - (\mathrm{SiCl}_{2}\mathrm{O})_{n} - \mathrm{SiCl}_{3}]{{< /katex >}} where {{< katex >}}n = 1-6{{< /katex >}}.

**Alcoholysis:** The chloride ion in silicon tetrachloride can be substituted by nucleophiles such as OH, OR etc. using suitable reagents. For example, it forms silicic esters with alcohols.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{SiCl}_{4} + 4\mathrm{C}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{5}\mathrm{OH} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Si}(\mathrm{OC}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{5})_{4} + 4\mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}

**Ammonolysis:** Similarly silicon tetrachloride undergoes ammonolysis to form chlorosilanes.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{SiCl}_{4} + \mathrm{NH}_{3} \xrightarrow[\mathrm{Ether}]{330\mathrm{K}} \mathrm{Cl}_{3}\mathrm{Si} - \mathrm{NH} - \mathrm{SiCl}_{3} + 2\mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}

**Uses:**

1. Silicon tetrachloride is used in the production of semiconducting silicon.
2. It is used as a starting material in the synthesis of silica gel, silicic esters, and as a binder for ceramic materials.

#### 2.3.8 Silicones

Silicones or polysiloxanes are organosilicon polymers with the general empirical formula {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{R}_{2}\mathrm{SiO}){{< /katex >}}. Since their empirical formula is similar to that of ketones {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{R}_{2}\mathrm{CO}){{< /katex >}}, they were named "silicones". These silicones may be linear or cross-linked. Because of their very high thermal stability they are called high-temperature polymers.

**Preparation:** Generally silicones are prepared by the hydrolysis of dialkyldichlorosilanes {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{R}_{2}\mathrm{SiCl}_{2}){{< /katex >}} or diaryldichlorosilanes {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{Ar}_{2}\mathrm{SiCl}_{2}){{< /katex >}}, which are prepared by passing vapours of RCl or ArCl over silicon at {{< katex >}}570\ \mathrm{K}{{< /katex >}} with copper as a catalyst.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{RCl} + \mathrm{Si} \xrightarrow{\mathrm{Cu} / 570\mathrm{K}} \mathrm{R}_{2}\mathrm{SiCl}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

The hydrolysis of dialkylchlorosilanes {{< katex >}}\mathrm{R}_{2}\mathrm{SiCl}_{2}{{< /katex >}} yields a straight chain polymer which grows from both ends.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<!-- Figure: Hydrolysis of dialkylchlorosilanes -->

The hydrolysis of monoalkylchlorosilanes {{< katex >}}\mathrm{RSiCl}_{3}{{< /katex >}} yields a very complex cross-linked polymer. Linear silicones can be converted into cyclic or ring silicones when water molecules are removed from the terminal -OH groups.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<!-- Figure: Formation of cyclic silicones -->

**Types of silicones:**

**(i) Linear silicones:** They are obtained by the hydrolysis and subsequent condensation of dialkyl or diaryl silicon chlorides.

**(ii) Cyclic silicones:** These are obtained by the hydrolysis of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{R}_{2}\mathrm{SiCl}_{2}{{< /katex >}}.

**(iii) Cross-linked silicones:** They are obtained by hydrolysis of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{RSiCl}_{3}{{< /katex >}}.

**Properties:** The extent of cross-linking and nature of the alkyl group determine the nature of the polymer. They range from oily liquids to rubber-like solids. All silicones are water repellent. This property arises due to the presence of organic side groups that surround the silicon which makes the molecule look like an alkane. They are also thermal and electrical insulators. Chemically they are inert. Lower silicones are oily liquids whereas higher silicones with long chain structure are waxy solids. The viscosity of silicone oil remains constant and does not change with temperature and they do not thicken during winter.

**Uses:**

1. Silicones are used for low temperature lubrication and in vacuum pumps, high temperature oil baths etc.
2. They are used for making waterproofing clothes.
3. They are used as insulating material in electrical motors and other appliances.
4. They are mixed with paints and enamels to make them resistant towards high temperature, sunlight, dampness and chemicals.

#### 2.3.9 Silicates

The minerals which contain silicon and oxygen in tetrahedral {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{SiO}_{4}]^{4-}{{< /katex >}} units linked together in different patterns are called silicates. Nearly 95% of the earth's crust is composed of silicate minerals and silica. The glass and ceramic industries are based on the chemistry of silicates.

**Types of Silicates:** Silicates are classified into various types based on the way in which the tetrahedral units {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{SiO}_{4}]^{4-}{{< /katex >}} are linked together.

**Orthosilicates (Nesosilicates):** The simplest silicates which contain discrete {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{SiO}_{4}]^{4-}{{< /katex >}} tetrahedral units are called orthosilicates or nesosilicates.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<!-- Figure 2.11 Structure of Orthosilicates -->

Examples: Phenacite - {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Be}_{2}\mathrm{SiO}_{4}{{< /katex >}} ({{< katex >}}\mathrm{Be}^{2+}{{< /katex >}} ions are tetrahedrally surrounded by {{< katex >}}\mathrm{O}^{2-}{{< /katex >}} ions), Olivine - {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{Fe}/\mathrm{Mg})_{2}\mathrm{SiO}_{4}{{< /katex >}} ({{< katex >}}\mathrm{Fe}^{2+}{{< /katex >}} and {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}{{< /katex >}} cations are octahedrally surrounded by {{< katex >}}\mathrm{O}^{2-}{{< /katex >}} ions).

**Pyrosilicates (Sorosilicates):** Silicates which contain {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{Si}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{7}]^{6-}{{< /katex >}} ions are called pyrosilicates (or sorosilicates). They are formed by joining two {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{SiO}_{4}]^{4-}{{< /katex >}} tetrahedral units by sharing one oxygen atom at one corner (one oxygen is removed while joining). Example: Thortveitite - {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Sc}_{2}\mathrm{Si}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{7}{{< /katex >}}.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<!-- Figure 2.12 Structure of Pyrosilicate -->

**Cyclic silicates (Ring silicates):** Silicates which contain {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{SiO}_{3})_{n}^{2n-}{{< /katex >}} ions formed by linking three or more tetrahedral {{< katex >}}\mathrm{SiO}_{4}^{4-}{{< /katex >}} units cyclically are called cyclic silicates. Each silicate unit shares two of its oxygen atoms with other units. Example: Beryl {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{Be}_{3}\mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SiO}_{3})_{6}]{{< /katex >}} (an aluminosilicate with each aluminium surrounded by 6 oxygen atoms octahedrally).

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<!-- Figure 2.13 Structure of Cyclic silicates -->

**Inosilicates:** Silicates which contain 'n' number of silicate units linked by sharing two or more oxygen atoms are called inosilicates. They are further classified as chain silicates and double chain silicates.

**Chain silicates (Pyroxenes):** These silicates contain {{< katex >}}[(\mathrm{SiO}_{3})_{n}]^{2n-}{{< /katex >}} ions formed by linking 'n' number of tetrahedral {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{SiO}_{4}]^{4-}{{< /katex >}} units linearly. Each silicate unit shares two of its oxygen atoms with other units. Example: Spodumene - {{< katex >}}\mathrm{LiAl}(\mathrm{SiO}_{3})_{2}{{< /katex >}}.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<!-- Figure 2.14 Structure of Chain silicates -->

**Double chain silicates (Amphiboles):** These silicates contain {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{Si}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{11}]_{n}^{6n-}{{< /katex >}} ions. In these silicates there are two different types of tetrahedra: (i) those sharing 3 vertices, (ii) those sharing only 2 vertices.

**Asbestos:** These are fibrous and noncombustible silicates. Therefore they are used for thermal insulation material, brake linings, construction material and filters. Asbestos being carcinogenic silicates, their applications are restricted.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<!-- Figure 2.15 Structure of Double chain silicates -->

**Sheet or phyllosilicates:** Silicates which contain {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{Si}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{5})_{n}^{2n-}{{< /katex >}} are called sheet or phyllosilicates. In these, each {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{SiO}_{4}]^{4-}{{< /katex >}} tetrahedron unit shares three oxygen atoms with others, thus forming two-dimensional sheets. These sheet silicates form layered structures in which silicate sheets are stacked over each other. The attractive forces between these layers are very weak, hence they can be cleaved easily just like graphite. Example: Talc, Mica etc.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<!-- Figure 2.16 Structure of Sheet or phyllosilicates -->

**Three-dimensional silicates (Tectosilicates):** Silicates in which all the oxygen atoms of {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{SiO}_{4}]^{4-}{{< /katex >}} tetrahedra are shared with other tetrahedra to form a three-dimensional network are called three-dimensional or tectosilicates. They have the general formula {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{SiO}_{2})_{n}{{< /katex >}}.

Examples: Quartz. These tectosilicates can be converted into three-dimensional aluminosilicates by replacing {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{SiO}_{4}]^{4-}{{< /katex >}} units by {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{AlO}_{4}]^{5-}{{< /katex >}} units. E.g., Feldspar, Zeolites etc.

#### 2.3.10 Zeolites

Zeolites are three-dimensional crystalline solids containing aluminium, silicon, and oxygen in their regular three-dimensional framework. They are hydrated sodium aluminosilicates with the general formula {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{O} \cdot (\mathrm{Al}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3})_{x} (\mathrm{SiO}_{2})_{y} \cdot \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}{{< /katex >}} ({{< katex >}}x = 2 \text{ to } 10{{< /katex >}}; {{< katex >}}y = 2 \text{ to } 6{{< /katex >}}).

Zeolites have a porous structure in which the monovalent sodium ions and water molecules are loosely held. The Si and Al atoms are tetrahedrally coordinated with each other through shared oxygen atoms. Zeolites are similar to clay minerals but they differ in their crystalline structure.

Zeolites have a three-dimensional crystalline structure that looks like a honeycomb consisting of a network of interconnected tunnels and cages. Water molecules move freely in and out of these pores but the zeolite framework remains rigid. Another special aspect of this structure is that the pore/channel sizes are nearly uniform, allowing the crystal to act as a molecular sieve. We have already discussed in XI standard the removal of permanent hardness of water using zeolites.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400)
<!-- Figure: Zeolite structure -->

**Boron Neutron Capture Therapy:** The affinity of Boron-10 for neutrons is the basis of a technique known as boron neutron capture therapy (BNCT) for treating patients suffering from brain tumours.

It is based on the nuclear reaction that occurs when boron-10 is irradiated with low-energy thermal neutrons to give high linear energy {{< katex >}}\alpha{{< /katex >}} particles and a Li particle.

Boron compounds are injected into a patient with a brain tumour and the compounds collect preferentially in the tumour. The tumour area is then irradiated with thermal neutrons and results in the release of an alpha particle that damages the tissue in the tumour each time a boron-10 nucleus captures a neutron. In this way damage can be limited preferentially to the tumour, leaving the normal brain tissue less affected. BNCT has also been studied as a treatment for several other tumours of the head and neck, the breast, the prostate, the bladder, and the liver.

## Summary

- The elements in which their last electron enters the 'p' orbital constitute the p-block elements. The p-block elements have a general electronic configuration of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{ns}^{2}, \mathrm{np}^{1-6}{{< /katex >}}. The elements of each group have similar outer shell electronic configuration and differ only in the value of {{< katex >}}n{{< /katex >}} (principal quantum number).
- Generally on descending a group the ionisation energy decreases and hence the metallic character increases. The ionisation enthalpy of elements in successive groups is higher than the corresponding elements of the previous group as expected.
- As we move down the {{< katex >}}13^{\mathrm{th}}{{< /katex >}} group, the electronegativity first decreases from boron to aluminium and then marginally increases.
- In p-block elements, the first member of each group differs from the other elements of the corresponding group.
- In heavier post-transition metals, the outer s electrons (ns) have a tendency to remain inert and show reluctance to take part in bonding, which is known as the inert pair effect.
- Some elements exist in more than one crystalline or molecular forms in the same physical state. For example, carbon exists as diamond and graphite. This phenomenon is called allotropism.
- Borax is a sodium salt of tetraboric acid. It is obtained from colemanite ore by boiling its solution with sodium carbonate.
- Boric acid can be extracted from borax and colemanite. Boric acid has a two-dimensional layered structure.
- The name alum is given to the double salt of potassium aluminium sulphate {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{Al}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} \cdot 24\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}]{{< /katex >}}.
- Carbon is found in the native form as graphite. Silicon occurs as silica (sand and quartz crystal). Silicate minerals and clay are other important sources for silicon.
- Catenation is the ability of an element to form chains of atoms.
- Carbon nanotubes, another recently discovered allotrope, have graphite-like tubes with fullerene ends.
- Silicones or polysiloxanes are organosilicon polymers with the general empirical formula {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{R}_{2}\mathrm{SiO}){{< /katex >}}. Because of their very high thermal stability they are called high-temperature polymers.
- The minerals which contain silicon and oxygen in tetrahedral {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{SiO}_{4}]^{4-}{{< /katex >}} units linked together in different patterns are called silicates.
- Types of Silicates: Orthosilicates (Nesosilicates), Pyrosilicates (Sorosilicates), Cyclic silicates (Ring silicates), Inosilicates (Chain silicates and Double chain silicates), Sheet or phyllosilicates, Three-dimensional silicates (Tectosilicates).
- Zeolites are three-dimensional crystalline solids containing aluminium, silicon, and oxygen in their regular three-dimensional framework. Zeolites act as molecular sieves for the removal of permanent hardness of water.
