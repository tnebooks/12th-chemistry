# p-BLOCK ELEMENTS - II

![](https://placehold.co/600x400) <!-- Figure: Sir William Ramsay (1852-1916) -->

## Learning Objectives

After studying this unit, the students will be able to

- discuss the preparation and properties of important compounds of nitrogen and phosphorus
- describe the preparation and properties of important compounds of oxygen and sulphur
- describe the preparation, properties of halogens and hydrogen halides
- explain the chemistry of inter-halogen compounds
- describe the occurrence, properties and uses of noble gases
- appreciate the importance of p-block elements and their compounds in day today life.

## 1.1 Occurrence

About {{< katex >}}78\%{{< /katex >}} of earth atmosphere contains dinitrogen {{< katex >}}\mathrm{(N_{2})}{{< /katex >}} gas. It is also present in earth crust as sodium nitrate (Chile saltpetre) and potassium nitrate (Indian saltpetre). The 11th most abundant element phosphorus, exists as phosphate (fluoroapatite, chloroapatite and hydroxyapatite). The other elements arsenic, antimony and bismuth are present as sulphides and are not very abundant.

### 3.1.3 Nitrogen

#### Preparation

Nitrogen, the principal gas of atmosphere ({{< katex >}}78\%{{< /katex >}} by volume) is separated industrially from liquid air by fractional distillation.

Pure nitrogen gas can be obtained by the thermal decomposition of sodium azide at about 575 K

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{NaN}_{3} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{Na} + 3\mathrm{N}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

It can also be obtained by oxidising ammonia using bromine water

{{< katex display=true >}}
8\mathrm{NH}_{3} + 3\mathrm{Br}_{2} \longrightarrow 6\mathrm{NH}_{4}\mathrm{Br} + \mathrm{N}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Properties

Nitrogen gas is rather inert. Terrestrial nitrogen contains {{< katex >}}14.5\%{{< /katex >}} and {{< katex >}}0.4\%{{< /katex >}} of nitrogen-14 and nitrogen-15 respectively. The later is used for isotopic labelling. The chemically inert character of nitrogen is largely due to high bonding energy of the molecules 225 cal mol{{< katex >}}^{-1}{{< /katex >}} (946 kJ mol{{< katex >}}^{-1}{{< /katex >}}). Interestingly the triply bonded species is notable for its less reactivity in comparison with other iso-electronic triply bonded systems such as –C=C–, C=O, X–C≡N, X–N=C, –C≡C–, and –C≡N. These groups can act as donor whereas dinitrogen cannot. However, it can form complexes with metal ({{< katex >}}\mathrm{M} \leftarrow \mathrm{N}{=}\mathrm{N}{{< /katex >}}) like CO to a less extent.

The only reaction of nitrogen at room temperature is with lithium forming {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Li}_{3}\mathrm{N}{{< /katex >}}. With other elements, nitrogen combines only at elevated temperatures. Group 2 metals and Th forms ionic nitrides.

{{< katex display=true >}}
6\mathrm{Li} + \mathrm{N}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{Li}_{3}\mathrm{N}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{Ca} + \mathrm{N}_{2} \xrightarrow{\text{red hot}} \mathrm{Ca}_{3}\mathrm{N}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{B} + \mathrm{N}_{2} \xrightarrow{\text{bright red hot}} 2\mathrm{BN}
{{< /katex >}}

Direct reaction with hydrogen gives ammonia. This reaction is favoured by high pressures and at optimum temperature in presence of iron catalyst. This reaction is the basis of Haber's process for the synthesis of ammonia.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\frac{1}{2}\mathrm{N}_{2} + \frac{3}{2}\mathrm{H}_{2} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{NH}_{3} \quad \Delta \mathrm{H}_{f} = -46.2\ \mathrm{kJ}\ \mathrm{mol}^{-1}
{{< /katex >}}

With oxygen, nitrogen produces nitrous oxide at high temperatures. Even at 3473 K nitrous oxide yield is only {{< katex >}}4.4\%{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{N}_{2} + \mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{N}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Uses of nitrogen

1. Nitrogen is used for the manufacture of ammonia, nitric acid and calcium cyanamide etc.
2. Liquid nitrogen is used for producing low temperature required in cryosurgery, and so in biological preservation.

### 3.1.4 Ammonia {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{NH}_{3}){{< /katex >}}

#### Preparation

Ammonia is formed by the hydrolysis of urea.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{NH}_{2}\mathrm{CONH}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{NH}_{3} + \mathrm{CO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

Ammonia is prepared in the laboratory by heating an ammonium salt with a base.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{NH}_{4}^{+} + \mathrm{OH}^{-} \longrightarrow \mathrm{NH}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{NH}_{4}\mathrm{Cl} + \mathrm{CaO} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CaCl}_{2} + 2\mathrm{NH}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

It can also be prepared by heating a metal nitrides such as magnesium nitride with water.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Mg}_{3}\mathrm{N}_{2} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 3\mathrm{Mg}(\mathrm{OH})_{2} + 2\mathrm{NH}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

It is industrially manufactured by passing nitrogen and hydrogen over iron catalyst (a small amount of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{O}{{< /katex >}} and {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Al}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3}{{< /katex >}} is also used to increase the rate of attainment of equilibrium) at {{< katex >}}750\ \mathrm{K}{{< /katex >}} at {{< katex >}}200\ \mathrm{atm}{{< /katex >}} pressure. In the actual process the hydrogen required is obtained from water gas and nitrogen from fractional distillation of liquid air.

#### Properties

Ammonia is a pungent smelling gas and is lighter than air. It can be readily liquefied at about 9 atmospheric pressure. The liquid boils at {{< katex >}}-38.4^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}} and freezes at {{< katex >}}-77^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}}. Liquid ammonia resembles water in its physical properties. i.e. it is highly associated through strong hydrogen bonding. Ammonia is extremely soluble in water (702 Volume in 1 Volume of water) at {{< katex >}}20^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}} and {{< katex >}}760\ \mathrm{mm}{{< /katex >}} pressure.

At low temperatures two soluble hydrate {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NH}_{3},\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}{{< /katex >}} and {{< katex >}}2\mathrm{NH}_{3},\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}{{< /katex >}} are isolated. In these molecules ammonia and water are linked by hydrogen bonds. In aqueous solutions also ammonia may be hydrated in a similar manner and we call the same as {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{NH}_{3},\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}){{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{NH}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{NH}_{4}^{+} + \mathrm{OH}^{-}
{{< /katex >}}

The dielectric constant of ammonia is considerably high to make it a fairly good ionising solvent like water.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{NH}_{3} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{NH}_{4}^{+} + \mathrm{NH}_{2}^{-}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{K}_{-50^{\circ}\mathrm{C}} = [\mathrm{NH}_{4}^{+}][\mathrm{NH}_{2}^{-}] = 10^{-30}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{O}^{+} + \mathrm{OH}^{-}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{K}_{25^{\circ}\mathrm{C}} = [\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{O}^{+}][\mathrm{OH}^{-}] = 10^{-14}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Chemical Properties

**Action of heat:** Above {{< katex >}}500^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}} ammonia decomposes into its elements. The decomposition may be accelerated by metallic catalysts like Nickel, Iron. Almost complete dissociation occurs on continuous sparking.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{NH}_{3} \xrightarrow{>500^{\circ}\mathrm{C}} \mathrm{N}_{2} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

**Reaction with air/oxygen:** Ammonia does not burn in air but burns freely in free oxygen with a yellowish flame to give nitrogen and steam.

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{NH}_{3} + 3\mathrm{O}_{2} \rightleftharpoons 2\mathrm{N}_{2} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

In presence of catalyst like platinum, it burns to produce nitric oxide. This process is used for the manufacture of nitric acid and is known as Ostwald's process.

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{NH}_{3} + 5\mathrm{O}_{2} \rightleftharpoons 4\mathrm{NO} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**Reducing property:** Ammonia acts as a reducing agent. It reduces the metal oxides to metal when passed over heated metallic oxide.

{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{PbO} + 2\mathrm{NH}_{3} \longrightarrow 3\mathrm{Pb} + \mathrm{N}_{2} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**Reaction with acids:** When treated with acids it forms ammonium salts. This reaction shows that the affinity of ammonia for proton is greater than that of water.

**Reaction with chlorine and chlorides:** Ammonia reacts with chlorine and chlorides to give ammonium chloride as a final product. The reactions are different under different conditions as given below.

With excess ammonia

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{NH}_{3} + 3\mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{N}_{2} + 6\mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
6\mathrm{HCl} + 6\mathrm{NH}_{3} \longrightarrow 6\mathrm{NH}_{4}\mathrm{Cl}
{{< /katex >}}

With excess of chlorine ammonia reacts to give nitrogen trichloride, an explosive substance.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{NH}_{3} + 6\mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{NCl}_{3} + 6\mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{NH}_{3}(\mathrm{g}) + \mathrm{HCl}(\mathrm{g}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{NH}_{4}\mathrm{Cl}(\mathrm{s})
{{< /katex >}}

**Formation of amides and nitrides:** With strong electro positive metals such as sodium, ammonia forms amides while it forms nitrides with metals like magnesium.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{Na} + 2\mathrm{NH}_{3} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{NaNH}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{Mg} + 2\mathrm{NH}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Mg}_{3}\mathrm{N}_{2} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

**With metallic salts:** Ammonia reacts with metallic salts to give metal hydroxides (in case of Fe) or forming complexes (in case Cu)

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Fe}^{3+} + 3\mathrm{NH}_{4}^{+} + 3\mathrm{OH}^{-} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Fe}(\mathrm{OH})_{3} + 3\mathrm{NH}_{4}^{+}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Cu}^{2+} + 4\mathrm{NH}_{3} \longrightarrow [\mathrm{Cu}(\mathrm{NH}_{3})_{4}]^{2+} \quad \text{(Tetraamminecopper(II) ion, a coordination complex)}
{{< /katex >}}

**Formation of amines:** Ammonia forms ammonated compounds by ion dipole attraction. Eg. {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{CaCl}_{2}.8\mathrm{NH}_{3}]{{< /katex >}}. In this, the negative ends of ammonia dipole is attracted to {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}{{< /katex >}} ion.

It can also act as a ligand and form coordination compounds such as {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{Co}(\mathrm{NH}_{3})_{6}]^{3+}{{< /katex >}}, {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{Ag}(\mathrm{NH}_{3})_{2}]^{+}{{< /katex >}}.

For example when excess ammonia is added to aqueous solution copper sulphate a deep blue colour compound {{< katex >}}[\mathrm{Cu}(\mathrm{NH}_{3})_{4}]^{2+}{{< /katex >}} is formed.

#### Structure of ammonia

Ammonia molecule is pyramidal in shape N–H bond distance is 1.016 Å and H–H bond distance is 1.645 Å with a bond angle {{< katex >}}107^{\circ}{{< /katex >}}. The structure of ammonia may be regarded as a tetrahedral with one lone pair of electrons in one tetrahedral position hence it has a pyramidal shape as shown in the figure.

### 3.1.5 Nitric acid

#### Preparation

Nitric acid is prepared by heating equal amounts of potassium or sodium nitrate with concentrated sulphuric acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{KNO}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{KHSO}_{4} + \mathrm{HNO}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

The temperature is kept as low as possible to avoid decomposition of nitric acid. The acid condenses to a fuming liquid which is coloured brown by the presence of a little nitrogen dioxide which is formed due to the decomposition of nitric acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{NO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{O}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Commercial method of preparation

Nitric acid prepared in large scales using Ostwald's process. In this method ammonia from Haber's process is mixed with about 10 times of air. This mixture is preheated and passed into the catalyst chamber where they come in contact with platinum gauze. The temperature rises to about 1275 K and the metallic gauze brings about the rapid catalytic oxidation of ammonia resulting in the formation of NO, which then oxidised to nitrogen dioxide.

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{NH}_{3} + 5\mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{NO} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + 120\ \mathrm{kJ}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{NO} + \mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{NO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

The nitrogen dioxide produced is passed through a series of adsorption towers. It reacts with water to give nitric acid. Nitric oxide formed is bleached by blowing air.

{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{NO}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{HNO}_{3} + \mathrm{NO}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Properties

Pure nitric acid is colourless. It boils at {{< katex >}}86^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}}. The acid is completely miscible with water forming a constant boiling mixture ({{< katex >}}98\%{{< /katex >}} {{< katex >}}\mathrm{HNO}_{3}{{< /katex >}}, boiling point {{< katex >}}120.5^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}}). Fuming nitric acid contains oxides of nitrogen. It decomposes on exposure to sunlight or on being heated, into nitrogen dioxide, water and oxygen.

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{NO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{O}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

Due to this reaction pure acid or its concentrated solution becomes yellow on standing.

In most of the reactions, nitric acid acts as an oxidising agent. Hence the oxidation state changes from {{< katex >}}+5{{< /katex >}} to a lower one. It doesn't yield hydrogen in its reaction with metals. Nitric acid can act as an acid, an oxidizing agent and an nitrating agent.

**As an acid:** Like other acids it reacts with bases and basic oxides to form salts and water

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{ZnO} + 2\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Zn}(\mathrm{NO}_{3})_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{FeO} + 10\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 3\mathrm{Fe}(\mathrm{NO}_{3})_{3} + \mathrm{NO} + 5\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**As an oxidising agent:** The nonmetals like carbon, sulphur, phosphorus and iodine are oxidised by nitric acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{C} + 4\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + 4\mathrm{NO}_{2} + \mathrm{CO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{S} + 2\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + 2\mathrm{NO}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{P}_{4} + 20\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{PO}_{4} + 4\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + 20\mathrm{NO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{I}_{2} + 10\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 6\mathrm{HIO}_{3} + 10\mathrm{NO} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HNO}_{3} + \mathrm{F}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{HF} + \mathrm{NO}_{3}\mathrm{F}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{S} + 2\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 3\mathrm{S} + 2\mathrm{NO} + 4\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**As a nitrating agent:** In organic compounds replacement of a {{< katex >}}-\mathrm{H}{{< /katex >}} atom with {{< katex >}}-\mathrm{NO}_{2}{{< /katex >}} is often referred as nitration. For example.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{C}_{6}\mathrm{H}_{6} + \mathrm{HNO}_{3} \xrightarrow{\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}} \mathrm{C}_{6}\mathrm{H}_{5}\mathrm{NO}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

Nitration takes place due to the formation of nitronium ion

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HNO}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{NO}_{2}^{+} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{HSO}_{4}^{-}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Action of nitric acid on metals

All metals with the exception of gold, platinum, rhodium, iridium and tantalum reacts with nitric acid. Nitric acid oxidises the metals. Some metals such as aluminium, iron, cobalt, nickel and chromium are rendered passive in concentrated acid due to the formation of a layer of their oxides on the metal surface, which prevents the nitric acid from reacting with pure metal.

With weak electropositive metals like tin, arsenic, antimony, tungsten and molybdenum, nitric acid gives metal oxides in which the metal is in the higher oxidation state and the acid is reduced to a lower oxidation state. The most common products evolved when nitric acid reacts with a metal are gases {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NO}_{2}{{< /katex >}}, NO and {{< katex >}}\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}{{< /katex >}}. Occasionally {{< katex >}}\mathrm{N}_{2}{{< /katex >}}, {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NH}_{2}\mathrm{OH}{{< /katex >}} and {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NH}_{3}{{< /katex >}} are also formed.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \xrightarrow{+5} \mathrm{NO}_{2} \xrightarrow{+4} \mathrm{HNO}_{2} \xrightarrow{+3} \mathrm{NO} \xrightarrow{+1} \mathrm{N}_{2}\mathrm{O} \xrightarrow{0} \mathrm{N}_{2} \xrightarrow{-3} \mathrm{NH}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

The reactions of metals with nitric acid are explained in 3 steps as follows:

**Primary reaction:** Metal nitrate is formed with the release of nascent hydrogen

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{M} + \mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{MNO}_{3} + (\mathrm{H})
{{< /katex >}}

**Secondary reaction:** Nascent hydrogen produces the reduction products of nitric acid.

**Tertiary reaction:** The secondary products either decompose or react to give final products

Decomposition of the secondary:

{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{HNO}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{HNO}_{3} + 2\mathrm{NO}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \quad \text{(Nitrous acid)}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{HNO}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{N}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \quad \text{(Nitrous acid)}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{N}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{N}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \quad \text{(Hyponitrous acid)}
{{< /katex >}}

Reaction of secondary products:

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HNO}_{2} + \mathrm{NH}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{N}_{2} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HNO}_{2} + \mathrm{NH}_{2}\mathrm{OH} \longrightarrow \mathrm{N}_{2}\mathrm{O} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HNO}_{2} + \mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{NO}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Examples

Copper reacts with nitric acid in the following manner

{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{Cu} + 6\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 3\mathrm{Cu}(\mathrm{NO}_{3})_{2} + 6(\mathrm{H})
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
6(\mathrm{H}) + 3\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 3\mathrm{HNO}_{2} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{HNO}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{HNO}_{3} + 2\mathrm{NO} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
Overall reaction:
{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{Cu} + 8\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 3\mathrm{Cu}(\mathrm{NO}_{3})_{2} + 2\mathrm{NO} + 4\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

The concentrated acid has a tendency to form nitrogen dioxide

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Cu} + 4\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Cu}(\mathrm{NO}_{3})_{2} + 2\mathrm{NO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

Magnesium reacts with nitric acid in the following way

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{Mg} + 8\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{Mg}(\mathrm{NO}_{3})_{2} + 8[\mathrm{H}]
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HNO}_{3} + 8(\mathrm{H}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{NH}_{3} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HNO}_{3} + \mathrm{NH}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{NH}_{4}\mathrm{NO}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}
Overall reaction:
{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{Mg} + 10\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{Mg}(\mathrm{NO}_{3})_{2} + \mathrm{NH}_{4}\mathrm{NO}_{3} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

If the acid is diluted we get {{< katex >}}\mathrm{N}_{2}\mathrm{O}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{Mg} + 10\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{Mg}(\mathrm{NO}_{3})_{2} + \mathrm{N}_{2}\mathrm{O} + 5\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Uses of nitric acid

1. Nitric acid is used as an oxidising agent and in the preparation of aquaregia.
2. Salts of nitric acid are used in photography ({{< katex >}}\mathrm{AgNO}_{3}{{< /katex >}}) and gunpowder for firearms ({{< katex >}}\mathrm{NaNO}_{3}{{< /katex >}}).

### 3.1.6 Oxides and oxoacids of nitrogen

### 3.1.7 Allotropic forms of phosphorus

Phosphorus has several allotropic modification of which the three forms namely white, red and black phosphorus are most common.

The freshly prepared white phosphorus is colourless but becomes pale yellow due to formation of a layer of red phosphorus upon standing. Hence it is also known as yellow phosphorus. It is poisonous in nature and has a characteristic garlic smell. It glows in the dark due to oxidation which is called phosphorescence. Its ignition temperature is very low and hence it undergoes spontaneous combustion in air at room temperature to give {{< katex >}}\mathrm{P}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{5}{{< /katex >}}.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400) <!-- Figure 3.2 Structure of white phosphorus -->

The white phosphorus can be changed into red phosphorus by heating it to {{< katex >}}420^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}} in the absence of air and light. Unlike white phosphorus it is not poisonous and does not show phosphorescence. It also does not ignite at low temperatures. The red phosphorus can be converted back into white phosphorus by boiling it in an inert atmosphere and condensing the vapour under water.

The black phosphorus has a layer structure and also acts as a semiconductor. The four atoms in phosphorus have polymeric structure with chains of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{P}_{4}{{< /katex >}} linked tetrahedrally. Unlike nitrogen {{< katex >}}\mathrm{P} \equiv \mathrm{P}{{< /katex >}} is less stable than P–P single bonds. Hence, phosphorus atoms are linked through single bonds rather than triple bonds. In addition to the above two more allotropes namely scarlet and violet phosphorus are also known for phosphorus.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400) <!-- Figure 3.3 Structure of red phosphorus -->

### 3.1.8 Properties of phosphorus

Phosphorus is highly reactive and has the following important chemical properties

**Reaction with oxygen:** Yellow phosphorus readily catches fire in air giving dense white fumes of phosphorus pentoxide. Red phosphorus also reacts with oxygen on heating to give phosphorus trioxide or phosphorus pentoxide.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{P}_{4} + 3\mathrm{O}_{2} \xrightarrow{\Delta} \mathrm{P}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{6}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{P}_{4} + 5\mathrm{O}_{2} \xrightarrow{\Delta} \mathrm{P}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{10}
{{< /katex >}}

**Reaction with chlorine:** Phosphorus reacts with chlorine to form tri- and pentachloride. Yellow phosphorus reacts violently at room temperature, while red phosphorus reacts on heating.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{P}_{4} + 6\mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{PCl}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{P}_{4} + 10\mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{PCl}_{5}
{{< /katex >}}

**Reaction with alkali:** Yellow phosphorus reacts with alkali on boiling in an inert atmosphere liberating phosphine. Here phosphorus acts as reducing agent.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{P}_{4} + 3\mathrm{NaOH} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 3\mathrm{NaH}_{2}\mathrm{PO}_{2} + \mathrm{PH}_{3} \uparrow
{{< /katex >}}

**Reaction with nitric acid:** When phosphorus is treated with conc. nitric acid it is oxidised to phosphoric acid. This reaction is catalysed by iodine crystals.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{P}_{4} + 20\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{PO}_{4} + 20\mathrm{NO}_{2} + 4\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**Reaction with metals:** Phosphorus reacts with metals like Ca and Mg to give phosphides. Metals like sodium and potassium react with phosphorus vigorously.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{P}_{4} + 6\mathrm{Mg} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{Mg}_{3}\mathrm{P}_{2} \quad \text{(Magnesium phosphide)}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{P}_{4} + 6\mathrm{Ca} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{Ca}_{3}\mathrm{P}_{2} \quad \text{(Calcium phosphide)}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{P}_{4} + 12\mathrm{Na} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{Na}_{3}\mathrm{P}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Uses of phosphorus

1. The red phosphorus is used in the match boxes.
2. It is also used for the production of certain alloys such as phosphor bronze.

### 3.1.9 Phosphine {{< katex >}}(\mathbf{PH}_{3}){{< /katex >}}

Phosphine is the most important hydride of phosphorus.

#### Preparation

Phosphine is prepared by action of sodium hydroxide with white phosphorus in an inert atmosphere of carbon dioxide or hydrogen.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{P}_{4} + 3\mathrm{NaOH} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 3\mathrm{NaH}_{2}\mathrm{PO}_{2} + \mathrm{PH}_{3} \uparrow
{{< /katex >}}
(sodium hypophosphite) (Phosphine)

Phosphine is freed from phosphine dihydride {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{P}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{4}){{< /katex >}} by passing through a freezing mixture. The dihydride condenses while phosphine does not.

Phosphine can also be prepared by the hydrolysis of metallic phosphides with water or dilute mineral acids.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Ca}_{3}\mathrm{P}_{2} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{PH}_{3} \uparrow + 3\mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{OH})_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{AlP} + 3\mathrm{HCl} \longrightarrow \mathrm{PH}_{3} \uparrow + \mathrm{AlCl}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

Phosphine is prepared in pure form by heating phosphorous acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{PO}_{3} \xrightarrow{\Delta} 3\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{PO}_{4} + \mathrm{PH}_{3} \uparrow
{{< /katex >}}

A pure sample of phosphine is prepared by heating phosphonium iodide with caustic soda solution.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{PH}_{4}\mathrm{I} + \mathrm{NaOH} \xrightarrow{\Delta} \mathrm{PH}_{3} \uparrow + \mathrm{NaI} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Physical properties

It is colourless, poisonous gas with rotten fish smell. It is slightly soluble in water and is neutral to litmus test. It condenses to a colourless liquid at {{< katex >}}188\ \mathrm{K}{{< /katex >}} and freezes to a solid at {{< katex >}}139.5\ \mathrm{K}{{< /katex >}}.

#### Chemical properties

**Thermal stability:** Phosphine decomposes into its elements when heated in absence of air at {{< katex >}}317\ \mathrm{K}{{< /katex >}} or when electric current is passed through it.

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{PH}_{3} \xrightarrow{317\ \mathrm{K}} \mathrm{P}_{4} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

**Combustion:** When phosphine is heated with air or oxygen it burns to give metaphosphoric acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{PH}_{3} + 8\mathrm{O}_{2} \xrightarrow{\Delta} \mathrm{P}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{10} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{P}_{4}\mathrm{O}_{10} + 6\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \xrightarrow{\Delta} 4\mathrm{HPO}_{3} + 4\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \quad \text{(Metaphosphoric acid)}
{{< /katex >}}

**Basic nature:** Phosphine is weakly basic and forms phosphonium salts with halogen acids.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{PH}_{3} + \mathrm{HI} \longrightarrow \mathrm{PH}_{4}\mathrm{I}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{PH}_{4}\mathrm{I} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{PH}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{O}^{+} + \mathrm{I}^{-}
{{< /katex >}}

It reacts with halogens to give phosphorus pentahalides.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{PH}_{3} + 4\mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{PCl}_{5} + 3\mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}

**Reducing property:** Phosphine precipitates some metal as phosphide from their salt solutions.

{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{AgNO}_{3} + \mathrm{PH}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Ag}_{3}\mathrm{P} + 3\mathrm{HNO}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

It forms coordination compounds with Lewis acids such as boron trichloride.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{BCl}_{3} + \mathrm{PH}_{3} \longrightarrow [\mathrm{Cl}_{3}\mathrm{B} \leftarrow \mathrm{PH}_{3}]
{{< /katex >}}

#### Structure

In phosphine, phosphorus shows {{< katex >}}\mathrm{sp}^{3}{{< /katex >}} hybridisation. Three orbitals are occupied by bond pair and fourth corner is occupied by lone pair of electrons. Hence, bond angle is reduced to {{< katex >}}93.5^{\circ}{{< /katex >}}. Phosphine has a pyramidal shape.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400) <!-- Figure 3.4 Structure of phosphine -->

#### Uses of phosphine

Phosphine is used for producing smoke screen as it gives large smoke. In a ship, a pierced container with a mixture of calcium carbide and calcium phosphide liberates phosphine and acetylene when thrown into sea. The liberated phosphine catches fire and ignites acetylene. These burning gases serves as a signal to the approaching ships. This is known as Holmes signal.

### 3.1.10 Phosphorus trichloride and pentachloride

#### Phosphorus trichloride

**Preparation:** When a slow stream of chlorine is passed over white phosphorus, phosphorus trichloride is formed. It can also be obtained by treating white phosphorus with thionyl chloride.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{P}_{4} + 8\mathrm{SOCl}_{2} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{PCl}_{3} + 4\mathrm{SO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{S}_{2}\mathrm{Cl}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

**Properties:** When phosphorus trichloride is hydrolysed with cold water it gives phosphorous acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{PCl}_{3} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{PO}_{3} + 3\mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}

This reaction involves the coordination of a water molecule using a vacant 3d orbital on the phosphorus atom followed by elimination of HCl which is similar to hydrolysis of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{SiCl}_{4}{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{PCl}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{PCl}_{3}\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{P}(\mathrm{OH})\mathrm{Cl}_{2} + \mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}

This reaction is followed by two more steps to give {{< katex >}}\mathrm{P}(\mathrm{OH})_{3}{{< /katex >}} or {{< katex >}}\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{PO}_{3}{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HPOCl}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{PO}_{2}\mathrm{Cl} + \mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{PO}_{2}\mathrm{Cl} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{PO}_{3} + \mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}

Similar reactions occur with other molecules that contain alcohols and carboxylic acids.

{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{C}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{5}\mathrm{OH} + \mathrm{PCl}_{3} \longrightarrow 3\mathrm{C}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{5}\mathrm{Cl} + \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{PO}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{C}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{5}\mathrm{COOH} + \mathrm{PCl}_{3} \longrightarrow 3\mathrm{C}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{5}\mathrm{COCl} + \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{PO}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

**Uses:** Phosphorus trichloride is used as a chlorinating agent and for the preparation of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{PO}_{3}{{< /katex >}}.

#### Phosphorus pentachloride

**Preparation:** When {{< katex >}}\mathrm{PCl}_{3}{{< /katex >}} is treated with excess chlorine, phosphorus pentachloride is obtained.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{PCl}_{3} + \mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{PCl}_{5}
{{< /katex >}}

**Chemical properties:** On heating phosphorus pentachloride, it decomposes into phosphorus trichloride and chlorine.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{PCl}_{5}(\mathrm{g}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{PCl}_{3}(\mathrm{g}) + \mathrm{Cl}_{2}(\mathrm{g})
{{< /katex >}}

Phosphorus pentachloride reacts with water to give phosphoryl chloride and orthophosphoric acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{PCl}_{5} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{PCl}_{3} + 2\mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{PCl}_{3} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{PO}_{4} + 3\mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}
Overall reaction:
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{PCl}_{5} + 4\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{PO}_{4} + 5\mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}

Phosphorus pentachloride reacts with metals to give metal chlorides. It also chlorinates organic compounds similar to phosphorus trichloride.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{Ag} + \mathrm{PCl}_{5} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{AgCl} + \mathrm{PCl}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Sn} + 2\mathrm{PCl}_{5} \longrightarrow \mathrm{SnCl}_{4} + 2\mathrm{PCl}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{C}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{5}\mathrm{OH} + \mathrm{PCl}_{5} \longrightarrow \mathrm{C}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{5}\mathrm{Cl} + \mathrm{HCl} + \mathrm{POCl}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{C}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{5}\mathrm{COOH} + \mathrm{PCl}_{5} \longrightarrow \mathrm{C}_{2}\mathrm{H}_{5}\mathrm{COCl} + \mathrm{HCl} + \mathrm{POCl}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

**Uses:** Phosphorus pentachloride is a chlorinating agent and is useful for replacing hydroxyl groups by chlorine atom.

### 3.2 Oxygen

#### Preparation

The atmosphere and water contain {{< katex >}}23\%{{< /katex >}} and {{< katex >}}83\%{{< /katex >}} by mass of oxygen respectively. Most of the world's rocks contain combined oxygen. Industrially oxygen is obtained by fractional distillation of liquefied air. In the laboratory, oxygen is prepared by one of the following methods.

The decomposition of hydrogen peroxide in the presence of catalyst {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{MnO}_{2}){{< /katex >}} or by oxidation with potassium permanganate.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{O}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
5\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{2} + 2\mathrm{MnO}_{4}^{-} + 6\mathrm{H}^{+} \longrightarrow 5\mathrm{O}_{2} + 8\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + 2\mathrm{Mn}^{2+}
{{< /katex >}}

The thermal decomposition of certain metallic oxides or oxoanions gives oxygen.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{HgO} \xrightarrow{\Delta} 2\mathrm{Hg} + \mathrm{O}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{BaO}_{2} \xrightarrow{\Delta} 2\mathrm{BaO} + \mathrm{O}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{KClO}_{3} \xrightarrow[\Delta]{\mathrm{MnO}_{2}} 2\mathrm{KCl} + 3\mathrm{O}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{KNO}_{3} \xrightarrow{\Delta} 2\mathrm{KNO}_{2} + \mathrm{O}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Properties

Under ordinary condition oxygen exists as a diatomic gas. Oxygen is paramagnetic. Like nitrogen and fluorine, oxygen forms strong hydrogen bonds. Oxygen exists in two allotropic forms namely dioxygen {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{O}_{2}){{< /katex >}} and ozone or trioxygen {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{O}_{3}){{< /katex >}}. Although negligible amounts of ozone occurs at sea level it is formed in the upper atmosphere by the action of ultraviolet light. In the laboratory ozone is prepared by passing electrical discharge through oxygen. At a potential of 20,000 V about {{< katex >}}10\%{{< /katex >}} of oxygen is converted into ozone; it gives a mixture known as ozonised oxygen. Pure ozone is obtained as a pale blue gas by the fractional distillation of liquefied ozonised oxygen.

![](https://placehold.co/600x400) <!-- Figure 3.8 Structure of ozone -->

The ozone molecule has a bent shape and symmetrical with delocalised bonding between the oxygen atoms.

#### Chemical properties

The chemical properties of oxygen and ozone differ vastly. Oxygen combines with many metals and non-metals to form oxides. With some elements such as s-block elements combination of oxygen occurs at room temperature. Some less reactive metals react when powdered finely and made to react exothermically with oxygen at room temperature but a lump of metal is unaffected under same condition. These finely divided metals are known as pyrophoric and when set the powder on fire, heat is liberated during a reaction.

On the other hand ozone is a powerful oxidizing agent and it reacts with many substances under conditions where oxygen will not react. For example, it oxidises potassium iodide to iodine. This reaction is quantitative and can be used for estimation of ozone.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{O}_{3} + 2\mathrm{KI} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{KOH} + \mathrm{O}_{2} + \mathrm{I}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

Ozone is commonly used for oxidation of organic compounds. In acidic solution ozone exceeds the oxidising power of fluorine and atomic oxygen. The rate of decomposition of ozone drops sharply in alkaline solution.

#### Uses

1. Oxygen is one of the essential components for the survival of living organisms.
2. It is used in welding (oxyacetylene welding).
3. Liquid oxygen is used as fuel in rockets etc.

### 3.2.1 Allotropic forms of sulphur

Sulphur exists in crystalline as well as amorphous allotropic forms. The crystalline form includes rhombic sulphur (α‑sulphur) and monoclinic sulphur (β‑sulphur). Amorphous allotropic form includes plastic sulphur (γ‑sulphur), milk of sulphur and colloidal sulphur.

Rhombic sulphur (α‑sulphur) is the only thermodynamically stable allotropic form at ordinary temperature and pressure. The crystals have a characteristic yellow colour and are composed of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{S}_{8}{{< /katex >}} molecules. When heated slowly above {{< katex >}}96^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}}, it converts into monoclinic sulphur. Upon cooling below {{< katex >}}96^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}} the β form converts back to α form. Monoclinic sulphur also contains {{< katex >}}\mathrm{S}_{8}{{< /katex >}} molecules in addition to small amount of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{S}_{6}{{< /katex >}} molecules. It exists as long needle-like prisms and is also called prismatic sulphur. It is stable between {{< katex >}}96^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}} – {{< katex >}}119^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}} and slowly changes into rhombic sulphur.

When molten sulphur is poured into cold water a yellow rubbery ribbon of plastic sulphur is produced. They are very soft and can be stretched easily. On standing (cooling slowly) it slowly becomes hard and changes to stable rhombic sulphur.

Sulphur also exists in liquid and gaseous states. At around {{< katex >}}140^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}} the monoclinic sulphur melts to form mobile pale yellow liquid called λ‑sulphur. The vapour over the liquid sulphur consists of {{< katex >}}90\%{{< /katex >}} of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{S}_{8}{{< /katex >}}, {{< katex >}}\mathrm{S}_{7}{{< /katex >}} and {{< katex >}}\mathrm{S}_{6}{{< /katex >}} and small amount of mixture of {{< katex >}}\mathrm{S}_{2}{{< /katex >}}, {{< katex >}}\mathrm{S}_{3}{{< /katex >}}, {{< katex >}}\mathrm{S}_{4}{{< /katex >}}, {{< katex >}}\mathrm{S}_{5}{{< /katex >}} molecules.

### 3.2.2 Sulphur dioxide

#### Preparation

**From sulphur:** A large-scale production of sulphur dioxide is done by burning sulphur in air. About {{< katex >}}6–8\%{{< /katex >}} of sulphur is oxidised to {{< katex >}}\mathrm{SO}_{3}{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{S} + \mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{SO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{S} + 3\mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{SO}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

**From sulphides:** When sulphide ores such as galena (PbS), zinc blende (ZnS) are roasted in air, sulphur dioxide is liberated. Large amounts of sulphur dioxide required for manufacturing of sulphuric acid and other industrial purposes is prepared by this method.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{ZnS} + 3\mathrm{O}_{2} \xrightarrow{\Delta} 2\mathrm{ZnO} + 2\mathrm{SO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
4\mathrm{FeS}_{2} + 11\mathrm{O}_{2} \xrightarrow{\Delta} 2\mathrm{Fe}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3} + 8\mathrm{SO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

**Laboratory preparation:** Sulphur dioxide is prepared in the laboratory by treating a metal or metal sulphite with sulphuric acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Cu} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CuSO}_{4} + \mathrm{SO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{SO}_{3}^{2-} + 2\mathrm{H}^{+} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{SO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Properties

Sulphur dioxide gas is found in volcanic eruptions. A large amount of sulphur dioxide gas is released into atmosphere from power plants using coal and oil and copper melting plants. It is a colourless gas with a suffocating odour. It is highly soluble in water and it is 2.2 times heavier than air. Sulphur dioxide can be liquefied (boiling point 263 K) at 2.5 atmospheric pressure and 288 K.

#### Chemical properties

Sulphur dioxide is an acidic oxide. It dissolves in water to give sulphurous acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{SO}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{3} \quad \text{(Sulphurous acid)}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{3} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{H}^{+} + \mathrm{SO}_{3}^{2-}
{{< /katex >}}

**Reaction with sodium hydroxide and sodium carbonate:** Sulphur dioxide reacts with sodium hydroxide and sodium carbonate to form sodium bisulphite and sodium sulphite respectively.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{SO}_{2} + \mathrm{NaOH} \longrightarrow \mathrm{NaHSO}_{3} \quad \text{(Sodium bisulphite)}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{SO}_{2} + \mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{CO}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{NaHSO}_{3} + \mathrm{CO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{NaHSO}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{SO}_{2} \quad \text{(Sodium sulphite)}
{{< /katex >}}

**Oxidising property:** Sulphur dioxide oxidises hydrogen sulphide to sulphur and magnesium to magnesium oxide.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{S} + \mathrm{SO}_{2} \longrightarrow 3\mathrm{S} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{Mg} + \mathrm{SO}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{MgO} + \mathrm{S}
{{< /katex >}}

**Reducing property:** As it can readily be oxidised, it acts as a reducing agent. It reduces chlorine to hydrochloric acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{SO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + 2\mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}

It also reduces potassium permanganate and dichromate to {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Mn}^{2+}{{< /katex >}} and {{< katex >}}\mathrm{Cr}^{3+}{{< /katex >}} respectively.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{KMnO}_{4} + 5\mathrm{SO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + 2\mathrm{MnSO}_{4} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{Cr}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{7} + 3\mathrm{SO}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{K}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + \mathrm{Cr}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**Reaction with oxygen:** Sulphur dioxide is oxidised to sulphur trioxide upon heating with oxygen at high temperature. This reaction is used for the manufacture of sulphuric acid by contact process.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{SO}_{2}(\mathrm{g}) + \mathrm{O}_{2}(\mathrm{g}) \xrightarrow[450^{\circ}\mathrm{C}]{\mathrm{V}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{5}} 2\mathrm{SO}_{3}(\mathrm{g})
{{< /katex >}}

**Bleaching action of sulphur dioxide:** In presence of water, sulphur dioxide bleaches coloured wool, silk, sponges and straw into colourless due to its reducing property.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{SO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + 2(\mathrm{H})
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{X}_{\text{Coloured}} + 2(\mathrm{H}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{XH}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

However, the bleached product (colourless) if allowed to stand in air is reoxidised by atmospheric oxygen to its original colour. Hence bleaching action of sulphur dioxide is temporary.

#### Uses

1. Sulphur dioxide is used in bleaching hair, silk, wool etc.
2. It can be used for disinfecting crops and plants in agriculture.

#### Structure of sulphur dioxide

In sulphur dioxide, sulphur atom undergoes {{< katex >}}\mathrm{sp}^{2}{{< /katex >}} hybridisation. A double bond arises between S and O due to pπ–dπ overlapping.

### 3.2.3 Sulphuric acid {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}){{< /katex >}}

#### Preparation

Sulphuric acid can be manufactured by lead chamber process, cascade process or contact process. Here we discuss the contact process.

#### Manufacture of sulphuric acid by contact process

The contact process involves the following steps:

i. Initially sulphur dioxide is produced by burning sulphur or iron pyrites in oxygen/air.
ii. Sulphur dioxide formed is oxidised to sulphur trioxide by air in the presence of a catalyst such as {{< katex >}}\mathrm{V}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{5}{{< /katex >}} or platinised asbestos.
iii. The sulphur trioxide is absorbed in concentrated sulphuric acid and produces oleum {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{S}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{7}){{< /katex >}}. The oleum is converted into sulphuric acid by diluting it with water.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{SO}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{S}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{7} \xrightarrow{\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}} 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}
{{< /katex >}}

To maximise the yield the plant is operated at 2 bar pressure and {{< katex >}}720\ \mathrm{K}{{< /katex >}}. The sulphuric acid obtained in this process is over {{< katex >}}96\%{{< /katex >}} pure.

#### Physical properties

Pure sulphuric acid is a colourless, viscous liquid (density: {{< katex >}}1.84\ \mathrm{g/mL}{{< /katex >}} at {{< katex >}}298\ \mathrm{K}{{< /katex >}}). High boiling point and viscosity of sulphuric acid is due to the association of molecules together through hydrogen bonding.

The acid freezes at {{< katex >}}283.4\ \mathrm{K}{{< /katex >}} and boils at {{< katex >}}590\ \mathrm{K}{{< /katex >}}. It is highly soluble in water and has strong affinity towards water and hence it can be used as a dehydrating agent. When dissolved in water, it forms mono {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}\cdot\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}){{< /katex >}} and dihydrates {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}\cdot 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}){{< /katex >}} and the reaction is exothermic.

The dehydrating property can also be illustrated by its reaction with organic compounds such as sugar, oxalic acid and formic acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{C}_{12}\mathrm{H}_{22}\mathrm{O}_{11} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow 12\mathrm{C} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}\cdot 11\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HCOOH} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CO} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}\cdot\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
(\mathrm{COOH})_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CO} + \mathrm{CO}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}\cdot\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \quad \text{(Oxalic acid)}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Chemical properties

Sulphuric acid is highly reactive. It can act as a strong acid and an oxidising agent.

**Decomposition:** Sulphuric acid is stable; however, it decomposes at high temperatures to sulphur trioxide.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{SO}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}

**Acidic nature:** It is a strong dibasic acid. Hence it forms two types of salts namely sulphates and bisulphates.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + \mathrm{NaOH} \longrightarrow \mathrm{NaHSO}_{4} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \quad \text{(Sodium bisulphate)}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + 2\mathrm{NaOH} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \quad \text{(Sodium sulphate)}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + 2\mathrm{NH}_{3} \longrightarrow (\mathrm{NH}_{4})_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \quad \text{(Ammonium sulphate)}
{{< /katex >}}

**Oxidising property:** Sulphuric acid is an oxidising agent as it produces nascent oxygen.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{SO}_{2} + (\mathrm{O})
{{< /katex >}}

It oxidises metals like copper and non-metals like carbon and sulphur.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Cu} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CuSO}_{4} + \mathrm{SO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{C} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{SO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{S} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow 3\mathrm{SO}_{2} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Test for sulphate/sulphuric acid

Dilute solution of sulphuric acid/aqueous solution of sulphates gives white precipitate (barium sulphate) with barium chloride solution. It can also be detected using lead acetate solution. Here a white precipitate of lead sulphate is obtained.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{BaCl}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{BaSO}_{4} \downarrow + 2\mathrm{HCl} \quad \text{(Barium sulphate, white precipitate)}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
(\mathrm{CH}_{3}\mathrm{COO})_{2}\mathrm{Pb} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{PbSO}_{4} \downarrow + 2\mathrm{CH}_{3}\mathrm{COOH} \quad \text{(Lead sulphate, white precipitate)}
{{< /katex >}}

##### Preparation of chlorine

Electrolysis of brine:

At anode: {{< katex >}}2\mathrm{Cl}^{-} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Cl}_{2} + 2\mathrm{e}^{-}{{< /katex >}}
At cathode: {{< katex >}}2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + 2\mathrm{e}^{-} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{2} + 2\mathrm{OH}^{-}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}

**Reaction with hydrogen sulphide, bromides and iodides:** Chlorine oxidises hydrogen sulphide to sulphur and liberates bromine and iodine from bromides and iodides. However, it does not oxidise fluorides.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{S} + \mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{HCl} + \mathrm{S}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Cl}_{2} + 2\mathrm{KBr} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{KCl} + \mathrm{Br}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Cl}_{2} + 2\mathrm{KI} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{KCl} + \mathrm{I}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

**Reaction with alkali:** Chlorine reacts with cold dilute alkali to give chloride and hypochlorite while with hot concentrated alkali chlorides and chlorates are formed.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Cl}_{2} + 2\mathrm{NaOH} \longrightarrow \mathrm{NaOCl} + \mathrm{NaCl} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
3\mathrm{Cl}_{2} + 6\mathrm{NaOH} \xrightarrow{\text{hot}} \mathrm{NaClO}_{3} + 5\mathrm{NaCl} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**Oxidising and bleaching action:** Chlorine is a strong oxidising and bleaching agent because of the nascent oxygen.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{HCl} + \mathrm{HOCl}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HOCl} \longrightarrow \mathrm{HCl} + (\mathrm{O})
{{< /katex >}}
Colouring matter + Nascent oxygen → Colourless oxidation product

The bleaching action of chlorine is permanent. It oxidises ferrous salts to ferric, sulphites to sulphates and hydrogen sulphide to sulphur.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{FeCl}_{2} + \mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{FeCl}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{FeSO}_{4} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + \mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Fe}_{2}(\mathrm{SO}_{4})_{3} + 2\mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{3} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + 2\mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Cl}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{S} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{HCl} + \mathrm{S}
{{< /katex >}}

**Preparation of bleaching powder:** Bleaching powder is produced by passing chlorine gas through dry slaked lime (calcium hydroxide).

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{OH})_{2} + \mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CaOCl}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

**Displacement redox reactions:** Chlorine displaces bromine from bromides and iodine from iodides.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Cl}_{2} + 2\mathrm{KBr} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{KCl} + \mathrm{Br}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Cl}_{2} + 2\mathrm{KI} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{KCl} + \mathrm{I}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

**Formation of addition compounds:** Chlorine forms addition products with sulphur dioxide, carbon monoxide and ethylene. It forms substituted products with alkanes/arenes.

##### Uses of chlorine

1. Purification of drinking water
2. Bleaching of cotton textiles, paper and rayon
3. Extraction of gold and platinum

### 3.3.2 Hydrochloric acid

#### Laboratory preparation

It is prepared by the action of sodium chloride and concentrated sulphuric acid.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{NaCl} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{NaHSO}_{4} + \mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{NaHSO}_{4} + \mathrm{NaCl} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} + \mathrm{HCl}
{{< /katex >}}

Dry hydrochloric acid is obtained by passing the gas through conc. sulphuric acid.

#### Properties

Hydrogen chloride is a colourless, pungent smelling gas, easily liquefied to a colourless liquid (boiling point 189 K) and frozen into a white crystalline solid (melting point 159 K). It is extremely soluble in water.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HCl}(\mathrm{g}) + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}(\mathrm{l}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{O}^{+} + \mathrm{Cl}^{-}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Chemical properties

Like all acids it liberates hydrogen gas from metals and carbon dioxide from carbonate and bicarbonate salts.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Zn} + 2\mathrm{HCl} \longrightarrow \mathrm{ZnCl}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Mg} + 2\mathrm{HCl} \longrightarrow \mathrm{MgCl}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{CO}_{3} + 2\mathrm{HCl} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{NaCl} + \mathrm{CO}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{CaCO}_{3} + 2\mathrm{HCl} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CaCl}_{2} + \mathrm{CO}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{NaHCO}_{3} + 2\mathrm{HCl} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{NaCl} + \mathrm{CO}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

It liberates sulphur dioxide from sodium sulphite.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{3} + 2\mathrm{HCl} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{NaCl} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{SO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

When three parts of concentrated hydrochloric acid and one part of concentrated nitric acid are mixed, Aqua regia (Royal water) is obtained. This is used for dissolving gold, platinum etc.

### 3.3.3 Trends in physical and chemical properties of hydrogen halides

#### Preparation

Direct combination is a useful means of preparing hydrogen chloride. The reaction between hydrogen and fluorine is violent while the reaction between hydrogen and bromine or hydrogen and iodine are reversible and do not produce pure forms.

#### Displacement reactions

Concentrated sulphuric acid displaces hydrogen chloride from ionic chlorides. At higher temperatures the hydrogen sulphate formed reacts with further ionic chloride. Displacement can be used for the preparation of hydrogen fluorides from ionic fluorides. Hydrogen bromide and hydrogen iodide are oxidised by concentrated sulphuric acid and cannot be prepared by this method.

#### Hydrolysis of phosphorus trihalides

Gaseous hydrogen halides are produced when water is added in drops to phosphorus trihalides except phosphorus trifluoride.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{PX}_{3} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{PO}_{3} + 3\mathrm{HX}
{{< /katex >}}

Hydrogen bromide may be obtained by adding bromine dropwise to a paste of red phosphorus and water while hydrogen iodide is conveniently produced by adding water dropwise to a mixture of red phosphorus and iodine.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{P} + 3\mathrm{X}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{PX}_{3}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{PX}_{3} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{PO}_{3} + 3\mathrm{HX} \quad (\text{where X = Br or I})
{{< /katex >}}

Any halogen vapours which escape with the hydrogen halide are removed by passing the gases through a column of moist red phosphorus.

#### From covalent hydrides

Halogens are reduced to hydrogen halides by hydrogen sulphide.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{S} + \mathrm{X}_{2} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{HX} + \mathrm{S}
{{< /katex >}}

Hydrogen chloride is obtained as a by-product of the reactions between hydrocarbons and halogens.

**Table 3.4 General properties**

| Property | HF | HCl | HBr | HI |
|----------|----|-----|-----|----|
| Bond dissociation enthalpy (kJ mol⁻¹) | +562 | +431 | +366 | +299 |
| % of ionic character | 43 | 13 | 7 | 7 |

In line with the decreasing bond dissociation enthalpy, the thermal stability of hydrogen halides decreases from fluoride to iodide. For example, hydrogen iodide decomposes at {{< katex >}}400^{\circ}\mathrm{C}{{< /katex >}} while hydrogen fluoride and hydrogen chloride are stable at this temperature.

At room temperature, hydrogen halides are gases but hydrogen fluoride can be readily liquefied. The gases are colourless but with moist air give white fumes due to the production of droplets of hydrochloric acid. In HF, due to the presence of strong hydrogen bond it has high melting and boiling points. This effect is absent in other hydrogen halides.

#### Acidic properties

The hydrogen halides are extremely soluble in water due to ionisation.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HX} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{3}\mathrm{O}^{+} + \mathrm{X}^{-} \quad (\mathrm{X} = \mathrm{F}, \mathrm{Cl}, \mathrm{Br}, \mathrm{I})
{{< /katex >}}

Solutions of hydrogen halides are therefore acidic and known as hydrohalic acids. Hydrochloric, hydrobromic and hydroiodic acids are almost completely ionised and are therefore strong acids but HF is a weak acid; i.e. {{< katex >}}0.1\ \mathrm{mM}{{< /katex >}} solution is only {{< katex >}}10\%{{< /katex >}} ionised, but in 5 M and 15 M solution HF is a stronger acid due to the equilibrium:

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{HF} + \mathrm{F}^{-} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{HF}_{2}^{-}
{{< /katex >}}

At high concentration, the equilibrium involving the removal of fluoride ions is important. Since it affects the dissociation of hydrogen fluoride and increases hydrogen ion concentration. Several stable salts {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NaHF}_{2}{{< /katex >}}, {{< katex >}}\mathrm{KHF}_{2}{{< /katex >}} and {{< katex >}}\mathrm{NH}_{4}\mathrm{HF}_{2}{{< /katex >}} are known. The other hydrogen halides do not form hydrogen dihalides.

Hydrohalic acids show typical acidic properties. They form salts with bases, and react with metals to give hydrogen. Moist hydrofluoric acid (not dry) rapidly reacts with silica and glass.

{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{SiO}_{2} + 4\mathrm{HF} \longrightarrow \mathrm{SiF}_{4} + 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}
\mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{SiO}_{3} + 6\mathrm{HF} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Na}_{2}\mathrm{SiF}_{6} + 3\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}
{{< /katex >}}

#### Oxidation

Hydrogen iodide is readily oxidised to iodine; hence it is a reducing agent.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{HI} \rightleftharpoons 2\mathrm{H}^{+} + \mathrm{I}_{2} + 2\mathrm{e}^{-}
{{< /katex >}}

Acidic solution of iodides is readily oxidised. A positive result is shown by liberation of iodine which gives a blue-black colouration with starch.

{{< katex display=true >}}
2\mathrm{HBr} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{Br}_{2} + \mathrm{SO}_{2}
{{< /katex >}}

But hydrogen iodide and ionic iodides are rapidly reduced by {{< katex >}}\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}{{< /katex >}} into {{< katex >}}\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{S}{{< /katex >}} and not into {{< katex >}}\mathrm{SO}_{2}{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}
8\mathrm{HI} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow 4\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + 4\mathrm{I}_{2} + \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{S}
{{< /katex >}}

Reducing property of hydrogen iodide can also be explained by its reaction with alcohols to give ethane. It converts nitric acid into nitrous acid and dinitrogen dioxide into ammonium.

Hydrogen chloride is unaffected by concentrated sulphuric acid but affected by only strong oxidising agents like {{< katex >}}\mathrm{MnO}_{2}{{< /katex >}}, potassium permanganate or potassium chlorate.

**Table 3.5 Trends**

| Property | Order |
|----------|-------|
| Reactivity of hydrogen | Decreases from fluorine to iodine |
| Stability | Decreases from HF to HI |
| Volatility of the hydrides | HF < HI < HBr < HCl |
| Thermal stability | HF > HCl > HBr > HI |
| Boiling point | HCl < HBr < HI < HF |
| Acid strength | Increases from HF to HI |

#### Properties of interhalogen compounds

i. The central atom will be the larger one.
ii. It can be formed only between two halogens and not more than two halogens.

#### Uses of noble gases

**Helium:** Used as a diluent in oxygen cylinders for deep sea diving, as a coolant in nuclear reactors, and for filling balloons and airships.

**Neon:** Used in neon signs and advertising displays.

**Argon:** Used as an inert atmosphere in welding and in incandescent bulbs.

**Krypton:** Used in fluorescent bulbs, flash bulbs and lasers.

**Xenon:** Emits an intense light in discharge tubes instantly. Due to this it is used in high speed electronic flash bulbs used by photographers.

**Radon:** Radioactive and used as a source of gamma rays. Radon gas is sealed in small capsules and implanted in the body to destroy malignant (cancer) growth.

## Summary

- About {{< katex >}}78\%{{< /katex >}} of earth atmosphere contains dinitrogen {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{N}_{2}){{< /katex >}} gas. It is also present in earth crust as sodium nitrate (Chile saltpetre) and potassium nitrate (Indian saltpetre).
- Nitrogen, the principal gas of atmosphere ({{< katex >}}78\%{{< /katex >}} by volume) is separated industrially from liquid air by fractional distillation.
- Ammonia is formed by the hydrolysis of urea.
- Nitric acid is prepared by heating equal amounts of potassium or sodium nitrate with concentrated sulphuric acid.
- In most of the reactions, nitric acid acts as an oxidising agent. Hence the oxidation state changes from {{< katex >}}+5{{< /katex >}} to a lower one. It does not yield hydrogen in its reaction with metals.
- The reactions of metals with nitric acid are explained in 3 steps: (i) Primary reaction: metal nitrate is formed with release of nascent hydrogen; (ii) Secondary reaction: nascent hydrogen produces reduction products of nitric acid; (iii) Tertiary reaction: secondary products either decompose or react to give final products.
- Phosphorus has several allotropic modifications of which the three forms – white, red and black phosphorus – are most common.
- Yellow phosphorus is poisonous and has a characteristic garlic smell. It glows in the dark due to oxidation – phosphorescence.
- Yellow phosphorus readily catches fire in air giving dense white fumes of phosphorus pentoxide.
- Phosphine is prepared by action of sodium hydroxide with white phosphorus in an inert atmosphere of carbon dioxide or hydrogen.
- Phosphine is used for producing smoke screen as it gives large smoke.
- When a slow stream of chlorine is passed over white phosphorus, phosphorus trichloride is formed.
- Phosphorus trichloride and phosphorus pentachloride are used as chlorinating agents.
- Oxygen is paramagnetic. It exists in two allotropic forms: dioxygen {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{O}_{2}){{< /katex >}} and ozone {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{O}_{3}){{< /katex >}}.
- Ozone is commonly used for oxidation of organic compounds.
- Sulphur exists in crystalline as well as amorphous allotropic forms. The crystalline forms include rhombic sulphur (α‑sulphur) and monoclinic sulphur (β‑sulphur). Amorphous forms include plastic sulphur (γ‑sulphur), milk of sulphur and colloidal sulphur.
- Sulphuric acid can be manufactured by lead chamber process, cascade process or contact process.
- When dissolved in water, it forms mono- {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}\cdot\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}){{< /katex >}} and dihydrates {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{SO}_{4}\cdot2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}){{< /katex >}} and the reaction is exothermic.
- Halogens are present in combined form as they are highly reactive.
- Chlorine is manufactured by the electrolysis of brine in electrolytic process or by oxidation of HCl by air in Deacon's process.
- Chlorine is a strong oxidising and bleaching agent because of the nascent oxygen.
- When three parts of concentrated hydrochloric acid and one part of concentrated nitric acid are mixed, aqua regia (Royal water) is obtained. This is used for dissolving gold, platinum etc.
- Hydrogen halides are extremely soluble in water due to ionisation.
- Each halogen combines with other halogens to form a series of compounds called interhalogen compounds.
- Fluorine reacts readily with oxygen and forms difluorine oxide {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{F}_{2}\mathrm{O}){{< /katex >}} and difluorine dioxide {{< katex >}}(\mathrm{F}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{2}){{< /katex >}} where it has a –1 oxidation state.
- All the noble gases occur in the atmosphere.
- They are extremely stable and have a small tendency to gain or lose electrons.
- Sodium perxenate is very much known for its strong oxidizing property.
- The inertness of noble gases is an important feature of their practical uses.
